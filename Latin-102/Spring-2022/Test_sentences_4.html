7

The sequence et ... et works like the English "both ... and."

10

11
sed nōn loquor dē nōbīs, dē illīs loquor.
There are two main verbs in this sentence (loquor ... loquor), but they are not connected by a coordinator. This common phenomenon is called asyndeton.
12
ego ex ipsā rē magnam capiō voluptātem.
Why is ego used here even though capiō clearly indicates who the subject is?
13
ego ad Caesarem ūnās Capuā litterās dedī.
An example of the flexibility of Latin word order.
14
tū enim ad mē scrīpsistī Coccēī respōnsum.
Coccēius is a person's name; it is GEN here.
15
anteā capiēbam ex officiō meō voluptātem.
Nothing too special here.
16
exercitum Apollōniā Dyrrachiōque mōvistī.
Apollōnia and Dyrrachium are the names of cities; they are both ABL here.
