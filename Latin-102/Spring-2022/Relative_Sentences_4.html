Latin 102
Temporal Relatives, Temporal clauses, etc.

1
cum Philogenēs Lāodicēam ad mē vēnisset, hās litterās dedī.
Philogenēs is the name of a certain Greek man. Lāodicēa is the name of a certain Greek city.
2
illīus temporis, dum in spē pāx fuit, ratiōnem nostram vidēs.
ratiōnem probably means something like "plan" here.
3
cum Brundisium versus īrēs ad Caesarem, vēnistī ad mē in Formiānum.
Brundisium is the name of a certain Italiam town. Formiānum is the name of a certain country estate. versus is a adv. that specifies movement "in the direction of" or "towards," rather than "to."
4
cum ad tē litterās dedissem, redditae mihi sunt litterae ā Pompeiō.
Pompeius is the name of a famous Roman general.
5
Servius cum esset apud mē, Cephaliō cum tuīs litterīs sextō Īdūs venit.
Servius is the name of a certain Roman man. Cephaliō is the name of a certain Greek man. Īdūs is an ACC of limit: "to the Ides."
6
nam cum ad tē sextō Nōnās darem, eīdem tabellāriō dedī etiam ad Sēstium.
Nōnās is ACC of limit: "to the Nones." Sēstius is the name of a certain Roman man.
7
tulit haec cīvitās, quoad necesse fuit, vestram dominātiōnem.
quoad is a temporal relative: "while."
8
in Cūmānō cum essem, venit ad mē, quod mihi pergrātum fuit, noster Hortēnsius.
Cūmānum is the name of a certain estate. Hortēnsius is the name of a certain Roman man. quod introduces a relative clause which has as its antecedent the whole main clause.
9
Tūberō cum in Āfricam vēnisset, invēnit in prōvinciā cum imperiō Attium Vārum.
Tūberō is the name of a certain Roman man. Likewise for Attius Vārus.
10
ubi hostēs ad lēgātōs exercitumque pervēnērunt, ūniversī sē ad pedēs prōiciunt.
ubi is a relative adverb; it is used temporally here: "when."
11
sed ubi signa legiōnum appropinquāre coepērunt, sēsē in proximōs montīs cōnferunt.
cōnferunt + ACC reflexive (sēsē) means "take x-self/selves"; in other words "go."
12
cum Caesar in Galliam vēnit, alterīus factiōnis prīncipēs erant Aeduī, alterīus Sēquanī.
