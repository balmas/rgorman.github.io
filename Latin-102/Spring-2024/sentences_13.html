Latin 301
Futures and other Unreals

1
Sed erunt omnia facilia, sī valēbis.
  • erunt: future
  • valēbis: future
2
Dē rē Piliae quod scrībis, erit mihi cūrae.
  • Dē: prep. + ABL(X) = "concerning/about X"
  • Piliae: PrN of a Roman, Pilia, Piliae, f.
  • quod scrībis: autonymous relative clause (has no expressed antecedent)
  • quod: see relative adjective patterns
  • cūrae: DAT(X) can = "a cause for X"
3
Vellem dictum esset ab eōdem etiam dē Diōne.
  • Vellem: = present unreal; potential/unreal expressions of will are often used to make the expression more polite/diffident: "I would like/would have liked"
  • dictum esset = ut dictum esset; finite complement clause = "that it would have been said/spoken." In this passage, dīcō is used very generally to mean "to discuss a topic." Thus, dictum esset ... dē Diōnē = "that a discussion would have been held about Dion."
  • ab: prep. + ABL(X) = "through the agency of/by X"
  • etiam: = "also"
  • dē: prep. + ABL(X) = "concerning/about X"
  • Diōne: PrN of a Greek, Diō, Diōnis, m.
4
Neque tū hoc dīcere audēbis nec, sī cupiās, licēbit.
cupiās is SUBJ with a potential sense.
5
Sed id facilius cōnsequar, sī ad mē in Ciliciam vēneris.
Cilicia is a place name. A verb such as vēnerīs normally takes a single OBJ. However, multiple OBJs are possible when a second OBJ restates/refines the first, for example, "he came to Rome to the senate."
6
Quā rē usque ad Nōnās Maiās tē in Formiānō exspectābō.
Formiānum is a place name.
7
Tē convēnissem, sī eō, quō cōnstituerās, venīre voluissēs.
The pluperfect subjunctive verbs indicate past unreal (also called past contrary-to-fact).
8
Plūra scrīberem, sī iam putārem libenter tē legere posse.
The imperfect subjunctive verbs indicate present unreal (or present contrary-to-fact).
9
Litterās ad eum scrīpsī, quās cum accēperis, faciēs, quod volēs.
quās cum accēperis is an example of clause interlacing. We will discuss.
10
Omnibus appāruit, nisi ille fuisset, Spartam futūram nōn fuisse.
fuisset and futūram fuisse are existential uses of sum; they are also past unreal (contrary to fact).
11
Hīs dē rēbus plūra ad tē in eā epistulā scrībam, quam ipsī Quīntō dabō.
quam introduces a comparison clause.
12
Plūribus verbīs tēcum agerem, nisi prō mē apud tē rēs ipsa loquerētur.
agerem and loquerētur are present unreal (contrary to fact).
13
Aliī, quasi corpus nūllum sit hominis, ita praeter animum nihil cūrant.
As a predicative adjective, nūllum can be translated as "non-exisitent." sit is present potential. With an ACC direct object, cūrant can be translated "care about."
14
Intereā, quae ad dignitātem tuam pertinēre arbitrābor, dīligenter cūrābō.
arbitrābor is the verb of an antonomous relative clause which is the OBJ of cūrābō.
15
Haec sī et agēs et sentiēs, eris nōn modo cōnsul, sed magnus cōnsul.
Nothing too tricky here.
16
Nunc, ut ad tē anteā scrīpsī, sī ad nōs vēneris, cōnsilium tōtīus reī capiēmus.
Fairly straightforward.
17
Cum illūc vēnerō intellēxerōque, quod negōtī sit, tum, ad quōs diēs reditūrus sim, scrībam ad tē.
quod negōtī sit is an indirect question.
18
Cum haec legēs, habēbimus cōnsulēs et omnia perspicere poteris dē Caesare, dē Pompēiō, dē ipsīs iūdiciīs.
dē Caesare, dē Pompēiō, dē ipsīs iūdiciīs is an example of asyndeton.
19
Sī quid habērem, quod ad tē scrīberem, facerem id plūribus verbīs et saepius, sed nunc quae sint negōtia, vidēs.
quid in this context means "anything." habērem and facerem are present unreal (contrary to fact). scrīberem is past potential. quae sint negōtia is an indirect question clause.
20
Nōs in Formiānō esse volumus usque ad prīdiē Nōnās Maiās, sed eō sī ante eam diem nōn vēneris, Rōmae tē fortasse vidēbō.
Formiānum is a place name.
