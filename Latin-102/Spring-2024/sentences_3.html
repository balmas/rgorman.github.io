Latin 102
Relative Adjectives and Adverbs

1
dē Siccā ita est, ut scrībis.
  • dē: prep. + ABL(X) = "concerning/about X"
  • Siccā: PrN of a Roman man, Sicca, Siccae, m.
  • ut: relative adverb = "as"
ut is a relative adverb meaning "in the same way as"; Sicca is the proper name of a certain Roman man.
2
dē Oppiō factum est, ut voluī.
ut is a relative adverb meaning "in the same way as"; Oppius is the proper name of a certain Roman man.
3
Basilus, ut imperātum est, facit.
ut is a relative adverb meaning "in the same way as"; Basilus is the proper name of a certain Roman man.
4
quā cōnstituerat, proficīscitur.
quā is a relative adverb meaning "by which route."
5
Domitius, ut audiō, in Cosānō est.
ut is a relative adverb meaning "in the same way as"; Domitius is the proper name of a certain Roman man. Cosānum is the name of a place.
6
dē Asiāticō itinere, ut imperāvistī, fēcī.
ut is a relative adverb meaning "in the same way as"; Asiāticus, -a, um is a proper adjective: Asiatic
7
quot hominum linguae, tot nōmina deōrum.
quot is a relative adjective meaning "how many"; it is plural and has the same form in all genders and cases.
8
habuit suum negōtium Gallus, ut scrībit.
ut is a relative adverb meaning "in the same way as."
9
ad Faberium, ut tibi placuit, litterās mīsī.
ut is a relative adverb meaning "in the same way as." Faberius is the proper name of a certain Roman man.
10
tantum erat, quantum hominēs loquēbantur.
quantum is a relative adjective meaning "how/as much."
11
dē domō et ōrātiōne Cūriōnis, ut scrībis, ita est.
ut is a relative adverb meaning "in the same way as." Cūriō is the proper name of a Roman man; it is GEN here.
12

quantum is a relative adjective meaning "how/as much."
13
respondī hominī, ut potuī, ut dēbuī, ut voluī.
Each instance of ut here is a relative adverb meaning "in the same way as."
14
tālis in hāc urbe, quālis Aristīdēs Athēnīs fuit.
quālis is a relative adjective meaning "what sort," "such a kind"; it is M S NOM here.
15
Caesar, ut cōnstituerat, in Ītaliam proficīscitur.
ut is a relative adverb meaning "in the same way as."
16
quō contenderat, pervenit iīsque locīs castra pōnit.
quō is a relative adverb meaning "whither/to where."
17
nōluit eum locum, unde Helvētiī discesserant, vacāre.
unde is a relative adverb meaning "whence/from where." Helvētiī is the proper name of a certain tribe, "the Helvetians."
18
mea quidem, ut ad tē saepius scrīpsī, haec sententia est.
ut is a relative adverb meaning "in the same way as."
19
reliquī in castra, unde prīdiē erant ēgressī, cōnfugiunt.
unde is a relative adverb meaning "whence/from where."
20
omnēs Uticam relinquunt et, quō imperātum est, trānseunt.
quō is a relative adverb meaning "whither/to where." Utica is the proper name of a certain city in Africa; it is ACC here.
21
habēre tālem amīcum volunt, quālēs ipsī esse nōn possunt.
A philosophica statement about the kind of friends many people want to have.
22

In Roman society, personal recommendations were very important.
23
dēdūxistī colōniam Casilīnum, quō Caesar ante dēdūxerat.
dēdūcō is the mot juste for founding a colony. Casilīnum is the proper name of a certain Italian town; it is ACC here.
24
eō, unde erat profectus, sē recipit litterāsque Athēnās mīsit.
sē recipit is an idiom: "he takes himself back" = "he goes back."
25

For tanta ... quanta we might say "as much ... as."
26

27
per fīnīs Aeduōrum in Lingonēs contendit, ubi duae legiōnēs hiemābant.
Aeduī and Lingonēs are proper names of certain peoples; the first is GEN here, the second ACC.
28
ita tantam eōrum multitūdinem nostrī interfēcērunt, quantum fuit diēī spatium.
For tantam ... quantum we might say something like "as great ... as was ..."
29
Helvētiōs, Tulingōs, Latovīcōs in fīnīs suōs, unde erant profectī, revertī iussit.
Helvētiī, Tulingī, and Latovīcī are proper names of certain peoples; they are all ACC here.
30
quō contenderat, pervēnit, eōdemque ut sequerentur, ad Tīmotheum et Īphicraten nūntium mīsit.
quō is a relative adverb meaining "whither/to where." ut here is not a relative but rather the subordinating conjuction introducing a finite complement clause. The two words nūntium mīsit "sent a message" here can be considered a phrasal verb. In this context, the words are the equivalent of "ordered." For this reason there is an extended verb frame: SBJ orders someone (ad + ACC) to do something (UT + SUBJ). Tīmotheus and Īphicratēs are proper names of certain Greeks; they are both ACC here.
31
ibi Lūcius Cottā interficitur cum maximā parte mīlitum.
reliquī sē in castra recipiunt, unde erant ēgressī.
Lūcius Cottā is the proper name of a certain Roman man. sē recipiunt is an idiom: "the take themselves back" = "they go back." unde is a relative adverb meaning "whence/from where."
32
interim, iī, quī ad alteram oppidī partem, ut suprā dēmōnstrāvimus, mūnītiōnis causā convēnerant, eō contendērunt.
ut is a relative adverb meaning "in the same way as/ as." The ABL causā has a specialized meaning: "for the sake of / because of." It is further specified by a GEN, here mūnītiōnis. Thus the combination here means "because of the fortification."
33
habēbās exercitum tantum, quantum tibi nōn senātus aut populus Rōmānus dederat, sed quantum tua libīdō cōnscrīpserat.
tantum ... quantum can be translated "as great ... as."
34
trānseunt Rhēnum nāvibus ratibusque trīgintā mīlibus passuum īnfrā locum, ubi pōns erat perfectus praesidiumque ab Caesare relictum.
Rhēnus is the proper name of a certain river. mīlibus is ABL of extent of space (more often expressed by the ACC). Thus trīgintā mīlibus passuum = "thirty thousands of paces" = "thirty miles." ubi is a relative adverb meaning "where."
