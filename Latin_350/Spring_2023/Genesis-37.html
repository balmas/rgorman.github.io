Latin 350
Joseph and his Brothers, part 1

1
Habitavit autem Jacob in terra Chanaan, in qua pater suus peregrinatus est.
  • Jacob: Hebrew proper names are often left uninflected in the Vulgate; they have only the one form in all cases. The Hebrew Yaʻăqôb means "heel-catcher."
  • Chanaan: a place name (Hbr. Kᵉnaʻan) meaning "lowland." It refers to certain land on the west side of the Jordan River.
2
Et hae sunt generationes ejus:
  • This sentence is somewhat misleading since most of what follows is dedicated to the story of a single son of Jacob.
3
Joseph cum sedecim esset annorum, pascebat gregem cum fratribus suis adhuc puer:
  • Joseph: a PN (Hbr. Yôwçêph, "[God] has added").
  • esset: in a temporal clause introduced by cum, the IMPERF SUBJ indicates the same aspect and time combination as an IMPERF IND.
  • sedecim ... annorum: While nominative case is most usual, it is not uncommon to find nouns in the genitive functioning as Predicate Nominals (PNom) dependent on a form of sum.
  • adhuc puer: a secondary predication. We can translate "while still a boy" or the like.
4
et erat cum filiis Balae et Zelphae uxorum patris sui:
  • Balae et Zelphae uxorum: Jacob had children with several women. Here are mentioned Bilhah (Hebr. Bilhâh, "timid/modest one") and Zilpah (Hebr. Zilpâh, "a trickling"). These women were, respectively, the servants of Rachel and Leah, Jacob's wives.
5
accusavitque fratres suos apud patrem crimine pessimo.
  • accusavit: the frame of asscuso here is: [SBJ accuses X of Y] where X is ACC and Y is ABL.
  • crimine pessimo: the details of Joseph's accusation are not specified.
6
Israël autem diligebat Joseph super omnes filios suos, eo quod in senectute genuisset eum:
  • Israël: Hebr. Yisrâʼêl, "God prevails." A second name for Jacob.
  • eo quod: when quod appears in its function as causal subordinator (as here), the matrix clause "can also contain a corresponding causal expression" (OLS II. 283). Here that corresponding expression is eo, "from that [fact]."
7
fecitque ei tunicam polymitam.
  • polymitam: "with many threads," often interpreted as "with many colors of threads."
8
Videntes autem fratres ejus quod a patre plus cunctis filiis amaretur, oderant eum, ne -c poterant ei quidquam pacifice loqui.
  • Videntes: a circumstantial participle. Compare the difference between "the boy swimming near the reef was eaten by the shark," and "swimming near the reef, the boy was eaten by the shark."
  • quod: beginning with Teutullian (d. c. 220 CE) Christian writers increasingly use quod + finite verb for indirect statement clauses. Earlier writers preferred ACC + infinive for this construction. quod functions as a complementizer and can be translated "that."
  • cunctis filiis: ABL of comparison; translate "than all the[other] children/sons."
  • amaretur: the Subjunctive mood probably indicates that the content of the clause is not vouched for by the author.
  • oderant: this verb has no present stem, and therefore forms built with the present stem take on "present-stem meanings." For example, the pluperfect has the meaning usually carried by the perfect (simple past or completed present).
9
Accidit quoque ut visum somnium referret fratribus suis:
  • Accidit: the relevant frame is [SBJ happened] where SBJ is an ut + Subjunctive clause. We can often translate "it happened that ..." where "it" is not a true subject, but an expletive (place-holder).
  • visum somnium: in Latin, dreams are "seen" while in English we "have" dreams. Thus we might translate visum somnium, "seen dream" as "a dream he had" or the like.
10
quae causa majoris odii seminarium fuit.
  • quae: Latin is confortable with relative words that refer to previous sentences; English does not like it. Translate quae as "this."
11
Dixitque ad eos: Audite somnium meum quod vidi:
  • Audite ... vidi: It is often unclear whether it is better to treat a direct quotation as a dependency of another clause or as a separate sentence.
12
putabam nos ligare manipulos in agro: et quasi consurgere manipulum meum, et stare, vestrosque manipulos circumstantes adorare manipulum meum.
  • manipulos: here "bundles" of harvested stalks of grain, "sheaves."
  • circumstantes: circumstantial participle or secondary predication, "standing around/ surrounding."
13
Responderunt fratres ejus:
  • An easy one.
14
Numquid rex noster eris?
  • Numquid: an interrogative sentence adverbial that has no easy equivalent in English. It indicates that the person asking the question has a strong expectation of a negative answer. We can translate something like "surely it's impossible that ..."
15
aut subjiciemur ditioni tuae?
  • subjiciemur: the frame of subjicio is [SBJ places X under Y] where X is ACC and Y is DAT. When, as here, the verb is passive, X becomes SBJ and Y remains DAT.
16
Haec ergo causa somniorum atque sermonum invidiae et odii fomitem ministravit.
  • Haec causa somniorum atque sermonum: an odd way of saying that Joseph's claims about his dreams were among the reasons his brothers hated him.
  • ministravit: "supplied."
17
Aliud quoque vidit somnium, quod narrans fratribus, ait:
  • Aliud: "another."
18
Vidi per somnium, quasi solem, et lunam, et stellas undecim adorare me.
  • per somnium: English would say "in a dream."
  • quasi: "as it were." The word refers to the uncertain quality of dreams.
19
Quod cum patri suo, et fratribus retulisset, increpavit eum pater suus, et dixit:
  • Quod: a relative that refers to a previous sentence is often translated with a demonstrative in English, "this/that."
  • retulisset: refero often means "bring [back] information."
20
Quid sibi vult hoc somnium quod vidisti?
  • vult: this verb (and more fully the phrase vult dicere, "want to say") can signify "mean" (def. I.A.1.e). As well as people, its SBJ can include words, events, actions, etc. as in "what does the crisis mean?"
  • sibi: the contribution of this word to the meaning is not clear.
21
num ego et mater tua, et fratres tui adorabimus te super terram?
  • num: an interrogative sentence adverbial that has no easy equivalent in English. It indicates that the person asking the question has a strong expectation of a negative answer. We can translate something like "surely it's impossible that ..."
  • adorabimus te super terram: the Hebrew original says "bow down to you to the earth." Jerome has chosen this rather odd way of translating.
22
Invidebant ei igitur fratres sui:
  • Invidebant: the frame of invideo is [SBJ envies X] where X is DAT.
23
pater vero rem tacitus considerabat.
  • tacitus: a secondary predication: "being quiet/in silence."
24
Cumque fratres illius in pascendis gregibus patris morarentur in Sichem, dixit ad eum Israël:
  • in pascendis gregibus: a gerundival clause, literally "in the flocks being pastured." English prefers the active "[in] pasturing the flocks."
  • Sichem: an indeclinable place name (Hebr. Shᵉkem, "ridge").
25
Fratres tui pascunt oves in Sichimis:
  • Sichimis: from this form we see that the place name Sichem is apparently plural as is not uncommon (compare Athenae).
26
veni, mittam te ad eos.
  • Simple enough.
27
Quo respondente, Praesto sum, ait ei:
  • Quo respondente: an ABL ABS clause.
  • Praesto: "at your service."
28
Vade, et vide si cuncta prospera sint erga fratres tuos, et pecora: et renuntia mihi quid agatur.
  • si ... sint: in the "best" writers of Classical Latin, si rarely introduces an indirect question.
29
Missus de valle Hebron, venit in Sichem:
  • Missus: a circumstantial participle.
  • Hebron: a proper place name (Hebr. Chebrôwn, "association").
  • Sichem: a proper place name. See above sentence 24.
30
invenitque eum vir errantem in agro, et interrogavit quid quaereret.
  • errantem: a secondary predication, "discovered him wandering ..."
31
At ille respondit:
  • Easy.
32
Fratres meos quaero:
  • Also easy.
33
indica mihi ubi pascant greges.
  • indica: the frame of indico is [SBJ shows X to Y] where X is an indirect question clause and Y is DAT.
34
Dixitque ei vir:
  • Easy.
35
Recesserunt de loco isto:
  • de: the preposition here has it less common meaning "from."
36
audivi autem eos dicentes: Eamus in Dothain.
  • dicentes: secondary predication, "heard them saying..."
  • Eamus: Pres Subj in main clause usually has volitive meaning.
  • Dothain: a proper place name (Hebr. Dôthân, meaning uncertain).
37
Perrexit ergo Joseph post fratres suos, et invenit eos in Dothain.
  • Nothing tricky here.
38
Qui cum vidissent eum procul, antequam accederet ad eos, cogitaverunt illum occidere:
  • Qui: when the antecedent of a relative expression occurs in an earlier sentence, English prefers to translate with a demonstrative, "they."
  • antequam, when, as here, written as a single word antequam functions as a subordinator, "before."
  • cogitaverunt: ≈ "made a plan."
39
et mutuo loquebantur:
  • mutuo: = "mutually"; "to each other."
40
Ecce somniator venit:
  • a famous pithy statement.
41
venite, occidamus eum, et mittamus in cisternam veterem:
  • occidamus ... mittamus: Pres Subj in a main clause usually indicates volitive.
42
dicemusque:
  • Too easy.
43
Fera pessima devoravit eum:
  • Also easy.
44
et tunc apparebit quid illi prosint somnia sua.
  • prosint: the frame of prosum is [SBJ benefits X] where X is DAT.
  • quid: this is best taken as an ACC of extent, "to what extent?"
45
Audiens autem hoc Ruben, nitebatur liberare eum de manibus eorum, et dicebat:
  • Audiens: circumstantial participle.
  • Ruben: a personal name (Hebr. Rᵉʼûwbên, "behold a son!").
  • de: = "from."
46
Non interficiatis animam ejus, ne -c effundatis sanguinem:
  • interficiatis ... effundatis: Pres Subj in a main clause usually = volitive.
  • animam: "kill his life" is not idiomatic English, but the meaning is clear.
47
sed projicite eum in cisternam hanc, quae est in solitudine, manusque vestras servate innoxias:
  • solitudine: ="wilderness."
  • servate: the frame of servo is [SBJ keeps X Y] where both X and Y are ACC. The function of Y here is called an Object Complement (OComp).
48
hoc autem dicebat, volens eripere eum de manibus eorum, et reddere patri suo.
  • volens: circumstantial participle.
  • de: = "from."
49
Confestim igitur ut pervenit ad fratres suos, nudaverunt eum tunica talari et polymita:
  • ut: a relative adverb meaning "when."
  • nudaverunt: the frame of nudo is [SBJ strips X of Y] where X is ACC and Y is ABL.
50
miseruntque eum in cisternam veterem, quae non habebat aquam.
  • Fairly easy.
51
Et sedentes ut comederent panem, viderunt Ismaëlitas viatores venire de Galaad, et camelos eorum portantes aromata, et resinam, et stacten in Aegyptum.
  • sedentes: circumstantial participle.
  • ut comederent ...: purpose clause.
  • Ismaëlitas: = "Ishmaelites," the proper name of a people (Hebr. Yishmâʻêʼlîy, "a descendant of Ishmael"). Ishmael was the first son of Abraham. Ismaelite is used to refer to Arabs.
  • de: = "from."
  • Galaad: = "Gilead," a proper name of a place (Hebr. Gilʻâd, "hard country").
  • portantes: a secondary predication ("they see ... their camels carrying ...").
52
Dixit ergo Judas fratribus suis:
  • Judas: a personal name (Hebr. Yᵉhûwdâh, "praised").
53
Quid nobis prodest si occiderimus fratrem nostrum, et celaverimus sanguinem ipsius?
  • prodest: the frame of prosum is [SBJ benefits X] where X is DAT.
  • quid: this is best taken as an ACC of extent, "to what extent?"
  • occiderimus ... celaverimus: SUBJ in a conditional clause = potential
54
melius est ut venundetur Ismaëlitis, et manus nostrae non polluantur:
  • Ismaëlitis: a proper name; see sentence 51.
55
frater enim et caro nostra est.
  • An easy one.
56
Acquieverunt fratres sermonibus illius.
  • Acquieverunt: acquiesco has this frame [SBJ gives assent to X] where X is DAT.
57
Et praetereuntibus Madianitis negotiatoribus, extrahentes eum de cisterna, vendiderunt eum Ismaëlitis, viginti argenteis:
  • praetereuntibus: ABL ABS.
  • Madianitis: "Midianites," the proper name of a people (Hebr. Midyânîy). This seems to be another name for the Ishmaelites.
  • extrahentes: circumstantial participle.
  • vendiderunt: the frame of venundo is [SBJ sells X to Y for/in return for Z] where X is ACC, Y is DAT and Z is ABL.
  • Ismaëlitis: a proper name of a people; see sentence 51.
58
qui duxerunt eum in Aegyptum.
  • qui: when the antecedent of a relative expression occurs in an earlier sentence, English prefers to translate with a demonstrative, "they."
59
Reversusque Ruben ad cisternam, non invenit puerum:
  • Reversus: the perfect passive deponent of reverto is deponent, "having returned."
  • Ruben: a personal name (Hebr. Rᵉʼûwbên, "behold a son!").
60
et scissis vestibus pergens ad fratres suos, ait:
  • scissis: ABL ABS.
61
Puer non comparet, et ego quo ibo?
  • comparet: compareo can mean "be visible," "be in existence," "be alive."
  • quo: "whither."
62
Tulerunt autem tunicam ejus, et in sanguine haedi, quem occiderant, tinxerunt: mittentes qui ferrent ad patrem, et dicerent:
  • mittentes: circumstantial participle.
  • qui ... ferrent ... dicerent: an atonymous relative clause.
63
Hanc invenimus:
  • Easy.
64
vide utrum tunica filii tui sit, an non. sit
  • utrum: a sentence adverb, "whether."
65
Quam cum agnovisset pater, ait:
  • agnovisset: Pluperf Subj in a cum clause = Pluperf Indic.
66
Tunica filii mei est:
  • Easy.
67
fera pessima comedit eum, bestia devoravit Joseph.
  • Easy enough.
68
Scissisque vestibus, indutus est cilicio, lugens filium suum multo tempore.
  • Scissis: ABL ABS.
  • lugens: circumstantial participle.
69
Congregatis autem cunctis liberis ejus ut lenirent dolorem patris, noluit consolationem accipere, sed ait:
  • Congregatis: ABL ABS.
  • ut lenirent ...: purpose clause.
70
Descendam ad filium meum lugens in infernum.
  • lugens: circumstantial participle.
71
Et illo perseverante in fletu, Madianitae vendiderunt Joseph in Aegypto Putiphari eunucho Pharaonis, magistro militum.
  • perseverante: ABL ABS.
  • Madianitae: the proper name of a people; see sentence 57.
  • Joseph: a personal name.
  • Putiphari: "Patiphar," a personal name.
