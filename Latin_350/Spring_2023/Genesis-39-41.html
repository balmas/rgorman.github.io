Latin 350
Joseph and his Brothers, part 2

1
Igitur Joseph ductus est in Aegyptum, emitque eum Putiphar eunuchus Pharaonis, princeps exercitus, vir aegyptius, de manu Ismaëlitarum, a quibus perductus erat.
  • Putiphar: Personal name (PN).
  • Ismaëlitarum: proper name of a group of people, "the Ishmaelites."
  • a quibus: the frame of perduco is [SBJ leads X to Y] where X is ACC and Y is an expression (e.g., an adverb or prepositional phrase) specifying a goal. When, as here, the verb is passive, X is "promoted" to SBJ. The agent (the entity that does the leading) can still be expressed using a AB + ABL prepositional phrase. Misleadingly, the Ancient Latin Treebank annotation guidelines mark such a structure as OBJ, In addition, in this example, the goal of perductus erat is not make explicit since it is clear from context (Egypt).
2
Fuitque Dominus cum eo, et erat vir in cunctis prospere agens:
  • cum eo: as we see here, other structures besides nouns or adjectives in the nominative may function as predicate "nominals".
  • Dominus: here = Yᵉhôvâh (Hebr. "the existing one"). The name Yᵉhôvâh itself is an intentional distortion from the nomen tetragrammaton = יהוה (YHVH ). These four letters represent the consonants in the true name of God. The true name was considered too sacred to say aloud, so different (incorrect) vowels were used.
3
habitavitque in domo domini sui, qui optime noverat Dominum esse cum eo, et omnia, quae gerebat, ab eo dirigi in manu illius.
  • domini ... Dominum: the first dominus refers to Joseph's human owner, the second to God. Such ambiguity must have sometimes been confusing before the introduction of the distinction between upper-case and lower-case.
  • ab eo: the anaphor seems to be to Potiphor.
  • illius: the anaphor seems to be to Joseph.
4
Invenitque Joseph gratiam coram domino suo, et ministrabat ei:
  • gratiam: = "favor." This is a complex concept. It refers to a relationship between people in which kindnessess are exchanged. For example, Joseph may do Potiphar's hard work, and in exchange, Potiphar may say "thanks." Hence Latin gratia can also mean "thanks" as in Spanish/Italian (gracias/grazie).
5
a quo praepositus omnibus gubernabat creditam sibi domum, et universa quae ei tradita fuerant:
  • praepositus: the frame of propono is [SBJ puts X in charge of Y] where X is ACC and Y is DAT. When the verb is passive, as here, X is "promoted" to SBJ and Y remains DAT. With the passive, the original SBJ may (some restrictions apply) be expressed with an AB + ABL prepositional phrase.
  • creditam: the frame of credo is [SBJ entrusts X to Y] where X is ACC and Y is DAT. When, as here, the verb is passive, X is "promoted" to SBJ (normally, the SBJ of an attributive participle is considered to be the word the participle is dependent upon) and Y remains DAT.
  • tradita: the frame of trado is [SBJ hands X over to Y] where X is ACC and Y is Dat. When, as here, the verb is passive, X is "promoted" to SBJ and Y remains DAT.
6
benedixitque Dominus domui Aegyptii propter Joseph, et multiplicavit tam in aedibus quam in agris cunctam ejus substantiam:
  • benedixit: the frame of benedico is [SBJ puts a blessing upon X] where X is DAT.
  • tam ... quam: = "both ... and."
  • substantiam: = "resources/wealth."
7
nec quidquam aliud noverat, nisi panem quo vescebatur. noverat
  • noverat: the SBJ of this verbs seems to be Potiphar, who left everything in the household to Joseph.
8
Erat autem Joseph pulchra facie, et decorus aspectu.
  • et: note the coordination of an ABL and a nominative. This link is evidence that ABL phrases can function as PNOM just as NOM phrases do.
  • pulchra facie: = "with a pretty face," ABL of quality.
  • aspectu: the ABL of a 4th declension noun derived from a verb is often called a "supine." Here we may call this meaning an ABL of specification since decorus aspectu = "beautiful to look at."
9
Post multos itaque dies injecit domina sua oculos suos in Joseph, et ait:
  • injecit: editions of later texts such as the Vulgate often print the consonantal i as j.
10
Dormi me cum.
  • An easy sentence.
11
Qui nequaquam acquiescens operi nefario, dixit ad eam:
  • acquiescens: the frame of acquiesco is [SBJ gives assent to X] where X is DAT.
12
Ecce dominus meus, omnibus mihi traditis, ignorat quid habeat in domo sua:
  • traditis: ABL ABS clause. The frame of trado is [SBJ hands over X to Y] where X is ACC and Y is DAT. When, as here, the verb is passive, X is "promoted" to SBJ and Y remains DAT.
  • ignorat: the frame of ignoro is [SBJ does not know X] where X is an expression representing what is not known. X can be a substantive or a clause.
13
nec quidquam est quod non in mea sit potestate, vel non tradiderit mihi, praeter te, quae uxor ejus es:
  • est: best taken here as existential rather than equational: = "there is not..."
  • in mea potestate: again we see a prepositional phrase functioning in the same way as a nominative noun/adjective (as PNOM).
  • tradiderit: a slightly ungrammatical structure since tradiderit continues the relative clause introduced by the nominative quod but quod cannot be the SBJ of tradiderit.
14
quomodo ergo possum hoc malum facere, et peccare in Deum meum?
  • peccare: the frame of pecco is [SBJ commits an offense against X] where X is an in + ACC prepositional phrase.
15
Hujusce modi verbis per singulos dies, et mulier molesta erat adolescenti:
  • Hujusce = huiusce = huis + -ce. The -ce adds greater emphasis to the adjective.
16
et ille recusabat stuprum.
  • stuprum: this word refers to sex that was thought to be dishonorable in some way. Here Potiphar's wife and Joseph would be committing adultery.
17
Accidit autem quadam die ut intraret Joseph domum, et operis quippiam absque arbitris faceret: et illa, apprehensa lacinia vestimenti ejus, diceret:
  • Accidit: the frame of accidit is [SBJ happened] where SBJ is an ut + SUBJN clause (English "that" clause).
  • operis quippiam: = "something of work"; a common Latin construction = "some work."
  • apprehensa: ABL ABS clause.
18
Dormi me cum.
  • See sentence #10.
19
Qui relicto in manu ejus pallio fugit, et egressus est foras.
  • relicto: ABL ABS clause.
  • foras: an example of an adverb functioning as the argument of a verb.
20
Cumque vidisset mulier vestem in manibus suis, et se esse contemptam, vocavit ad se homines domus suae, et ait ad eos:
  • vidisset: in temporal clauses introduced by cum, PLUPERF SUBJN specifies the same aspect/tense as PLUPERF IND.
  • vestem ... et se esse contemptam: the coordination (et) indicates that the noun and the infinitive clause have the same syntactic function.
21
En introduxit virum hebraeum, ut illuderet nobis:
  • En: a particle that is intended to raise the emotional impact of the sentence.
  • ut illuderet: when classified semantically, adverbial ut + SUBJN clauses (English "so that" or "that" clauses) are usually considered either purpose or result clauses.
  • illuderet: the frame of illudo is [SBJ brings ridicule upon X] where X is DAT.
22
ingressus est ad me, ut coiret me cum:
  • ut coiret: when classified semantically, adverbial ut + SUBJN clauses (English "so that" or "that" clauses) are usually considered either purpose or result clauses.
  • coiret: the frame of coeo is: [SBJ has sex with X] where X is a cum + ABL prepositional phrase.
23
cumque ego succlamassem, et audisset vocem meam, reliquit pallium quod tenebam, et fugit foras.
  • succlamassem et audisset: in temporal clauses introduced by cum, PLUPERF SUBJN specifies the same aspect/tense as PLUPERF IND.
24
In argumentum ergo fidei retentum pallium ostendit marito revertenti domum, et ait:
  • In argumentum: in + ACC can indicate "the object or end in view" (II.C.2); here = "for proof."
  • fidei: = "trustworthiness" (II.A).
25
Ingressus est ad me servus hebraeus quem adduxisti, ut illuderet mihi:
  • ut illuderet: see sentence 21.
26
cumque audisset me clamare, reliquit pallium quod tenebam, et fugit foras.
  • audisset: in temporal clauses introduced by cum, PLUPERF SUBJN specifies the same aspect/tense as PLUPERF IND.
27
His auditis dominus, et nimium credulus verbis conjugis, iratus est valde:
  • auditis ... et ... credulus: here two different types of functions are coordinated, a phenomenon that is generally avoided. The collocation here is presumably justified by the flexibility in meaning for Absolute Clauses and Secondary Predications. Both elements here indicated the reasons why Potiphar became so angry.
28
tradiditque Joseph in carcerem, ubi vincti regis custodiebantur, et erat ibi clausus.
  • tradidit: the frame of trado is [SUBJ hands over X to Y] where X is ACC and Y is a variety of expressions indicating location/goal/recepient (here: an in + ACC prepositional phrase).
  • vincti: best taken as a substantive (from the participle) = "prisoners."
29
Fuit autem Dominus cum Joseph, et misertus illius dedit ei gratiam in conspectu principis carceris.
  • misertus: the frame of misereo is [SBJ pities X] where X is GEN.
30
Qui tradidit in manu illius universos vinctos qui in custodia tenebantur:
  • tradidit: the frame of trado is [SUBJ hands over X to Y] where X is ACC and Y is a variety of expressions indicating location/goal/recepient (here: an in + ABL prepositional phrase).
31
et quidquid fiebat, sub ipso erat.
  • Not too complicated.
32
Nec noverat aliquid, cunctis ei creditis:
  • noverat: forms with the present stem of nosco, noscere, novi, notum mean "get to know/learn," while forms with the perfect stem (nov-) mean "know." Therefor the PLUPERF can be translated "knew" = "had got to know."
  • creditis: ABL ABS; the frame of credo is [SBJ entrusts X to Y] where X is ACC and Y is DAT. When, as here, the verb is passive, X is "promoted" to SBJ and Y remains DAT.
33
Dominus enim erat cum illo, et omnia opera ejus dirigebat.
  • No comment.
34
His ita gestis, accidit ut peccarent duo eunuchi, pincerna regis Aegypti, et pistor, domino suo.
  • gestis: ABL ABS.
  • Accidit: the frame of accidit is [SBJ happened] where SBJ is an ut + SUBJN clause (English "that" clause).
  • pistor: = "miller" or "baker."
35
Iratusque contra eos Pharao ( nam alter pincernis praeerat, alter pistoribus ), misit eos in carcerem principis militum, in quo erat vinctus et Joseph.
  • nam ... pistoribus: a parenthetical sentence is an independent sentence inserted into another sentence.
  • praeerat: the frame of praesum is [SBJ is in charge of X] where X is DAT.
36
At custos carceris tradidit eos Joseph, qui et ministrabat eis:
  • ministrabat: the frame of ministro is [SBJ manages/governs X] where X id DAY.
37
aliquantulum temporis fluxerat, et illi in custodia tenebantur.
  • aliquantulum temporis: = "a fair amount of time."
38
Videruntque ambo somnium nocte una, juxta interpretationem congruam sibi:
  • nocte una: = "on one night" = "on the same night."
  • juxta interpretationem congruam sibi: an obscure expression that seems to mean something like "with a different interpretation."
39
ad quos cum introisset Joseph mane, et vidisset eos tristes, sciscitatus est eos, dicens:
  • mane: = "in the morning."
40
Cur tristior est hodie solito facies vestra?
  • solito: = "than the accustomed thing" (ABL of comparison) = "than usual/customary."
41
Qui responderunt:
  • No comment.
42
Somnium vidimus, et non est qui interpretetur nobis.
  • interpretetur: IMPERF SUBJ here indicates potential in past time = "might interpret."
43
Dixitque ad eos Joseph:
  • No comment.
44
Numquid non Dei est interpretatio?
  • Numquid: an adverb expecting an negative answer to a question. Keep in mind here that the question itself is a negative (non ... est). Here two negatives make a positive.
45
referte mihi quid videritis.
  • referte: = "report."
  • videritis: in clauses of this type, the perfect subjnctive specifies the aspect/tense combination usually specified by the perfect indicative.
46
Narravit prior, praepositus pincernarum, somnium suum:
  • prior: best taken as a substantive here, = "the first one."
47
Videbam coram me vitem, in qua erant tres propagines, crescere paulatim in gemmas, et post flores uvas maturescere: calicemque Pharaonis in manu mea:
  • Nothing too tricky here.
48
tuli ergo uvas, et expressi in calicem quem tenebam, et tradidi poculum Pharaoni.
  • Again, fairly uncomplicated.
49
Respondit Joseph:
  • No comment.
50
Haec est interpretatio somnii:
  • No comment.
51
tres propagines, tres adhuc dies sunt:
  • No comment.
52
post quos recordabitur Pharao ministerii tui, et restituet te in gradum pristinum:
  • recordabitur: the frame of recordor is [SUBJ calls to mind X] where X is GEN.
  • restituet: the frame of restituo is [SBJ restores X to Y] where X is ACC and Y may come from a range of expressions indicating the goal.
53
dabisque ei calicem juxta officium tuum, sicut ante facere consueveras.
  • juxta: = "according to."
  • sicut: = "just as."
54
Tantum memento mei, cum bene tibi fuerit, et facias mecum misericordiam: ut suggeras Pharaoni ut educat me de isto carcere: quia furto sublatus sum de terra Hebraeorum, et hic innocens in lacum missus sum.
  • Tantum = "only/just."
  • memento: the frame of memini is [SBJ remembers X] where X is GEN.
  • facias: the Subjunctive here indicates volative and is thus the equivalent of an Imperative.
  • facias ... misericordiam: = "make/do pity" = "have pity." Jermome follows the Hebrew text closely in this expression.
  • mecum: = "with me" = "on me."
  • ut suggeras: this clause specifies just what the misericordia would consist of. We might translate "namely, that you suggest ..."
  • suggeras: the frame of suggero is [SBJ suggests X to Y] where X is an ut/ne + SUBJN clause and Y is DAT.
  • ut suggeras: according to traditional semantics-based terminology, this clause would be called an indirect command clause.
  • furto: = "by deception."
  • innocens: a secondary predication. Such structures are often best translated with a clause, for example, "although I was innocent."
55
Videns pistorum magister quod prudenter somnium dissolvisset, ait:
  • Videns: a circumstantial clause. video has the frame [SBJ sees that X] where X is an indirect statement/question clause.
  • quod ... dissolvisset: An indirect statement clause; this kind of clause is usually ACC + INF in classical Latin.
  • dissolvisset: in indirect statements introduced by quod, the PLUPERF SUBJ indivates the same aspect and time combination as would PLUPERF IND.
56
Et ego vidi somnium: quod tria canistra farinae dissolvisset super caput meum: et in uno canistro quod erat excelsius, portare me omnes cibos qui fiunt arte pistoria, avesque comedere ex eo.
  • quod ... haberem: this indirect statement clause is in apposition to, and further specifies, somnium.
  • dissolvisset: in an indirect statement clause introduced by quod, the IMPERF SUBJN indicates the same aspect and time combination as would the IMPERF IND.
  • portare: note that this clause is coordinated with the quod ... haberem clause.
  • fiunt: = "are made."
57
Respondit Joseph:
  • No comment.
58
Haec est interpretatio somnii:
  • No comment.
59
tria canistra, tres adhuc dies sunt:
  • No comment.
60
post quos auferet Pharao caput tuum, ac suspendet te in cruce, et lacerabunt volucres carnes tuas.
  • post quos: on the assumption that the relative expression post quos belongs to a separate sentence than the previous clause, we may translate "after these."
61
Exinde dies tertius natalitius Pharaonis erat:
  • No comment.
62
qui faciens grande convivium pueris suis, recordatus est inter epulas magistri pincernarum, et pistorum principis.
  • qui: = "he."
  • faciens: circumstance clause.
  • pueris suis: = "his servants"; as frequently in many cultures, people of low status were referred to as children, no matter their true age.
  • recordatus est: the frame of the deponent recordor is [SBJ calls to mind/remembers X] where X is GEN.
63
Restituitque alterum in locum suum, ut porrigeret ei poculum:
  • alterum = "the one."
  • ut porrigeret: according to semantic terminology, perhaps a purpose clause, "so that he might hold out ..."
64
alterum suspendit in patibulo, ut conjectoris veritas probaretur.
  • alterum: = "the other."
  • ut ... probaretur: according to semantic terminology, best considered a result clause, "so that the truth ... was proven."
65
Et tamen succedentibus prosperis, praepositus pincernarum oblitus est interpretis sui.
  • succedentibus: head of ABL ABS clause.
  • prosperis: a substantive adj. Here it means something like "times of good fortune."
  • praepositus: circumstantial participle.
  • oblitus est: the frame of obliviscor is [SBJ forgets X] where X is GEN.
66
Post duos annos vidit Pharao somnium.
  • No comment necessary.
67
Putabat se stare super fluvium, de quo ascendebant septem boves, pulchrae et crassae nimis:
  • stare: in this type of clause, the PRES INFIN indicates Progressive and no time; this aspect means that the action of stare is simultaneous with the time indicated by the matrix verb.
68
et pascebantur in locis palustribus.
  • No comment.
69
Aliae quoque septem emergebant de flumine, foetae confectaeque macie:
  • confectae: the relevant meaning of conficio is "to wear out/consume" (B.II).
70
et pascebantur in ipsa amnis ripa in locis virentibus:
  • No comment.
71
devoraveruntque eas, quarum mira species et habitudo corporum erat.
  • habitudo: the medical profession still speaks of the "habitus of the body," meaning "physique."
72
Expergefactus Pharao, rursum dormivit, et vidit alterum somnium:
  • Expergefactus: a circumstantial clause. Apparently Pharaoh woke up briefly between his two dreams.
73
septem spicae pullulabant in culmo uno plenae atque formosae:
  • No comment.
74
aliae quoque totidem spicae tenues, et percussae uredine oriebantur, devorantes omnium priorum pulchritudinem.
  • totidem: an indeclinable adj (here FEM PLUR NOM) = "just as many."
  • devorantes: circumstantial clause.
75
Evigilans Pharao post quietem, et facto mane, pavore perterritus, misit ad omnes conjectores Aegypti, cunctosque sapientes, et accersitis narravit somnium, nec erat qui interpretaretur.
  • Evigilans: circumstantial.
  • facto: ABL ABS; literally "with morning having become" = "when it was morning."
  • perterritus: circumstantial
  • misit: here the frame of mitto is [SBJ sends a message to X] where X is expressed by an ad + ACC prepositional phrase.
  • accersitis: it is relatively rare to find, as here, a participle used substantively.
  • qui interpretaretur: a headless (or autonymous) relative clause can usefully be thought of as the equivalent of an adjective used substantively.
76
Tunc demum reminiscens pincernarum magister, ait:
  • reminiscens: circumstantial
77
Confiteor peccatum meum:
  • No comment.
78
iratus rex servis suis, me et magistrum pistorum retrudi jussit in carcerem principis militum: ubi una nocte uterque vidimus somnium praesagum futurorum.
  • iratus: circumstantial. The frame of irascor is [SBJ becomes angry at X] where X is DAT.
  • jussit: the frame of jubeo is [SBJ orders X] where X is a complement clause.
  • futurorum: can be interpreted as a participle used substantively = "things about to exist."
79
Erat ibi puer hebraeus, ejusdem ducis militum famulus:
  • No comment.
80
cui narrantes somnia, audivimus quidquid postea rei probavit eventus;
  • cui: an anaphoric reference to a previous sentence so we may translate "to him."
  • narrantes: circumstantial.
  • rei ... eventus: = "the outcome of the matter" or the like.
81
ego enim redditus sum officio meo, et ille suspensus est in cruce.
  • redditus sum: the frame of reddo is [SBJ returns X to Y] where X is ACC and Y is DAT. Where, as here, the verb is passive, the ACC is promoted to SBJ and Y remains DAT.
82
Protinus ad regis imperium eductum de carcere Joseph totonderunt: ac veste mutata obtulerunt ei.
  • ad ... imperium: ad + ACC can mean "according to" (Defin. I.D.3.a).
  • eductum: circumstantial.
  • mutata: ABL ABS.
  • obtulerunt: the OBJ is understood anaphorically from the first part of the sentence: Joseph.
83
Cui ille ait:
  • Cui: anaphoric to previous sentence; we may translate "to him."
84
Vidi somnia, nec est qui edisserat:
  • qui edisserat: headless (or autonymous) relative clause.
85
quae audivi te sapientissime conjicere.
  • quae: anaphoric to somnia in previous sentence; we may translate "them."
  • conjicere: = "interpret."
86
Respondit Joseph:
  • No comment.
87
Absque me Deus respondebit prospera Pharaoni.
  • prospera: as a substantive = "good omens."
88
Narravit ergo Pharao quod viderat:
  • quod: anaphoric reference to somnium implicit in the preceding context.
89
Putabam me stare super ripam fluminis, et septem boves de amne conscendere, pulchras nimis, et obesis carnibus:
  • stare ... conscendere: in this kind of clause, the present infinitives indicated progressive/simultaneous aspect. Other tenses of the infinitive may also occur in this kind of clause.
  • de: de + ABL often = "from" in later Latin such as this.
  • pulchras ... carnibus: these atributive expressions probably are positioned here for emphasis.
90
quae in pastu paludis virecta carpebant.
  • quae: anaphor for boves.
91
Et ecce, has sequebantur aliae septem boves, in tantum deformes et macilentae, ut numquam tales in terra Aegypti viderim:
  • in tantum: = "to such an extent."
  • ut ... viderim: many grammarians would call this a result clause or a consecutive clause. In the functional terms used by the Oxford Latin Sybtax it is called a declarative noun clause.
  • viderim: here has completed present as it aspect and tense combination.
92
quae, devoratis et consumptis prioribus, nullum saturitatis dedere vestigium: sed simili macie et squalore torpebant.
  • quae: anaphor for boves.
  • devoratis: ABL ABS.
93
Evigilans, rursus sopore depressus, vidi somnium.
  • Evigilans: unusually, this present participle does not indicate action simultaneous with the matrix verb. The sequence of events is 1) pharoah woke up after dreaming of the cattle, 2) pharoah falls back to sleep, 3) pharoah dreams again.
94
Septem spicae pullulabant in culmo uno plenae atque pulcherrimae.
  • plenae atque pulcherrimae: again the attributes come at the end of the sentence probably for emphasis and effect.
95
Aliae quoque septem tenues et percussae uredine, oriebantur e stipula:
  • No comment.
96
quae priorum pulchritudinem devoraverunt.
  • quae: anaphor to spicae.
97
Narravi conjectoribus somnium, et nemo est qui edisserat.
  • qui edisserat: it is not clear whether this should be considered a headless relative clause and a PNOM rather than an ATR depending on nemo.
  • edisserat: the SUBJ is potential and generalizes; ≈ "could explain."
98
Respondit Joseph:
  • No comment.
99
Somnium regis unum est:
  • unum: a literal translation of the Hebr. ʼechâd; it indicates that the dreams have the same meaning.
100
quae facturus est Deus, ostendit Pharaoni.
  • quae facturus est Deus: a headless relative clause. There is no clear difference in meaning from an indirect question clause (which would have had the SUBJ facturus sit).
101
Septem boves pulchrae, et septem spicae plenae, septem ubertatis anni sunt:
  • No comment.
102
eamdemque vim somnii comprehendunt.
  • vim: = "meaning."
103
Septem quoque boves tenues atque macilentae, quae ascenderunt post eas, et septem spicae tenues, et vento urente percussae, septem anni venturae sunt famis.
  • venturae: prospective aspect and no time.
104
Qui hoc ordine complebuntur:
  • hoc ordine: = "in this order".
105
ecce septem anni venient fertilitatis magnae in universa terra Aegypti, quos sequentur septem anni alii tantae sterilitatis, ut oblivioni tradatur cuncta retro abundantia:
  • ut ... tradatur: as pointed out by OLS, the subjunctive is not semantically justified in a result clause (the traditional name for this sort of clause). We may translate with the simple present: "is handed over."
106
consumptura est enim fames omnem terram, et ubertatis magnitudinem perditura est inopiae magnitudo.
  • consumptura est ... perditura est: prospective and present, "SBJ is going to VERB."
107
Quod autem vidisti secundo ad eamdem rem pertinens somnium: firmitatis indicium est, eo quod fiat sermo Dei, et velocius impleatur.
  • quod ... vidisti: quod can be a subordinating conjunction introducing a declarative argument clause, "that you saw ..."
  • eo quod: can be understood as a causal clause, "from the [fact] that" ≈ "because."
  • fiat sermo: a strange expression in Latin, "the conversation is made." Here Jerome seems to follow his sources rather than looking for a more normal Latin phrasing. Hebr.: kî dābār nakhōn mim 'elōhīm ("because the utterence [is] established by God"); Greek: hoti alēthes estai to rhēma to para tou theou / ὅτι ἀληθὲς ἔσται τὸ ῥῆμα τὸ παρὰ τοῦ θεοῦ ("because the utterence from God will be true").

Fecitque eum ascendere super currum suum secundum, clamante praecone, ut omnes coram eo genu flecterent, et praepositum esse scirent universae terrae Aegypti.
  • fecit: facio here in the rare meaning "cause." The frame is [SBJ makes X Y] where X is ACC and Y is an infinitive verb.
  • ut ... flecterent et ... scirent: OLS would call these directive argument clauses; they are directive because the subjunctives may be understood to have volitive force (here expressing commands). Traditional grammars might call these clauses indirect commands.
  • praepositum esse: = perfect passive infinitive. The frame is [SBJ puts X in charge of Y] where X is ACC and Y is DAT. When passive, X becomes the SBJ and Y remains DAT.
