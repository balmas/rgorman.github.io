1
Audiens autem Jacob quod alimenta venderentur in Aegypto, dixit filiis suis:
  • venderentur: in clauses of this type (what type is it?) an imperfect subjunctive indicates the aspect and time combination usually indicated by the imperfect indicative.
2
Quare negligitis?
  • Quare: sometimes printed as two words = "why."
3
audivi quod triticum venundetur in Aegypto:
  • venundetur: in clauses of this type (what type is it?) a present subjunctive indicates the aspect and time combination usually indicated by the present indicative.
4
descendite, et emite nobis necessaria, ut possimus vivere, et non consumamur inopia.
  • nobis: the grammar of this word is uncertain. It may be directly dependent on emite or it may depend on necessaria. Be ready to translate each interpretation.
  • ut possimus vivere, et non consumamur: it is easiest to interpret these as purpose clauses. In Classical Latin the negative used in a purpose clause is ne rather than non, but this rule is no longer followed by Jerome's day. The subjunctive is mandatory in a purpose clause, and that mood is semantically harmonious: a subjunctive in a main clause can mean volitive and a purpose clause had directive illocutionary force (the action of the purpose clause is desired by the speaker).
5
Descendentes igitur fratres Joseph decem, ut emerent frumenta in Aegypto, Benjamin domi retento a Jacob, qui dixerat fratribus ejus: Ne forte in itinere quidquam patiatur mali: ingressi sunt terram Aegypti cum aliis qui pergebant ad emendum.
  • Descendentes: Genesis regularly speaks of "descending" into Egypt.
  • emerent: purpose clause.
  • ejus: anaphor to Benjamin.
  • patiatur: purpose clause (negated).
  • emendum: a gerund = "buying." The preposition ad + ACC gerund is regularly used to express purpose.

13

  • recordatus: the frame of recordor is [SBJ remembers X] where X is GEN. We often translate the perfect participle of a deponent with the formula [having + -EN(verb], which indicates completed aspect, active voice, and no time. So recordatus = "having remembered."

22
immunita terrae hujus considerare venistis.
  • considerare: = "to examine"; this is a purpose clause--a new use of the infinitive for us. An infinitive as purpose clause is avoided by Cicero and Caesar, but does occur in earlier and later authors. Such a structure is generally restricted to matrix verbs indicating motion such as coming and going.

30

  • Mittite ... adducat: both volitive. Note how an imperative can be parallel to a present subjunctive.
31

  • probentur: subjunctives are common is temporal clauses introduced by donc = "until."
  • utrum vera an falsa sint: an odd way of putting it. We would expect "SBJ may be proved true or false." Instead, we find "SBJ may be proved whether they are true or false."

  • tribus diebus: = "for three days." The use of the bare ablative (i.e., ablative w/o any preposition) to indicated extent of time is rare in early and classical Latin. It be comes relatively common from the 1st century CE."

  • ad invicem: an adverb as the object of a preposition is rare in Latin = "to each other."
