The discussion begins. "Cicero" and his young interlocutor start a disputatio about a matter of life and death.

Translate these sentences into English and be ready to explain your work.

1
Malum mihi esse mors videtur.
  • Malum: best taken as a substantive here, = "an evil"
  • videtur: video = "see"; in the passive it often = "seem"
2
Is -ne, qui mortui sunt, an is, quibus moriendum est?
  • Is = iīs: when used adverbially, DAT(X) can indicate for whom the action or situation holds true.
  • quibus: when dependent on a gerundive, DAT(X) indicates the agent for whom the obligation holds: = "gerundive(G) must be carried out by (DAT(X).
  • moriendum est: when a transitive verb is used in the gerundive, the normal rules of passivization apply, and what would be the direct object of the active appears as the SBJ of the passive. When the verb is intransitive, as here, the verb is used "impersonally." In such cases, the verb becomes, in a way, its own SBJ. So moriendum est means something like "a death must be died" or "an act of dying must be carried out." This not satisfactory English. It is often preferable to make the agent of a gerundive the SBJ of an active verb.
3
Utrisque.
  • Utrisque: when used adverbially, DAT(X) can indicate for whom the action or situation holds true. = "for both."
4
Est miserum igitur, quoniam malum.
  • miserum: This adjective indicates the subjective psychological state that follows from the presence of malum, an objective "evil."
  • Note that ellipsis is common in conversation (and its literary representation). The verb of the quoniam clause is not expressed but is implicit by context.
5
Certe.
