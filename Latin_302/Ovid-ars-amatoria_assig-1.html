xx

Ars Amatoria



Ovid promises that his "hook-up manual" will provide expert knowledge. It will also make his name famous as the master-craftsman of romantic relationships.





Sī quis in hōc artem populō nōn nōvit amandī, Hoc legat et lectō carmine doctus amet. Arte citae vēlōque ratēs rēmōque moventur, Arte levēs currūs: arte regendus amor. Curribus Automedōn lentīsque erat aptus habēnīs,      5 Tīphys in Haemoniā puppe magister erat: Mē Venus artificem tenerō praefēcit Amōrī; Tīphys et Automedōn dīcar Amōris ego.







Ovid rejects the traditional sources of knowledge for a poet. He has gained his expertise by the scientific method: trial and error.








Nōn ego , Phoebe , datās ā tē mihi mentiar artēs, Nec nōs āëriae vōce monēmur avis,            10 Nec mihi sunt vīsae Clīō Clīūsque sorōrēs Servantī pecudēs vallibus, Ascra, tuīs: Ūsus opus movet hoc: vātī pārēte perītō; Vēra canam: coeptīs, māter Amōris, ades ! Este procul, vittae tenuēs, insigne pudōris,      15 Quaeque tegis mediōs, instita longa, pedēs. Nōs Venerem tūtam concessaque furta canēmus, Inque meō nūllum carmine crīmen erit.











Ovid enumerates the labores associated with a relationship.
The parts of his poem will correspond to these labors.









Principiō, quod amāre velīs, reperīre labōrā, Quī nova nunc prīmum mīles in arma venīs.      20 Proximus huic labor est placitam exōrāre puellam: Tertius, ut longō tempore dūret amor. Hic modus, haec nostrō signābitur ārea currū: Haec erit admissā mēta premenda rotā.













The first step is to understand the best locations to find a partner.






Dum licet et lōrīs passim potes īre solūtīs,      25 Ēlige cui dīcās 'tū mihi sōla placēs.' Haec tibi nōn tenuēs veniet dēlapsa per aurās; Quaerenda est oculīs apta puella tuīs. Scit bene vēnātor, cervīs ubi rētia tendat; Scit bene, quā frendēns valle morētur aper;      30 Aucupibus nōtī fruticēs; quī sustinet hamōs, Nōvit quae multō pisce natentur aquae: Tū quoque, māteriam longō quī quaeris amōrī, Ante frequēns quō sit disce puella locō. Nōn ego quaerentem ventō dare vēla iubēbō,        35 Nec tibi , ut inveniās , longa terenda via est.





Gargara quot segetēs , quot habet Mēthymna racēmōs, Aequore quot piscēs, fronde teguntur avēs, Quot caelum stēllās, tot habet tua Rōma puellās: Māter in Aenēae cōnstitit urbe suī.                   40 Seu caperis prīmīs et adhūc crēscentibus annīs, Ante oculōs veniet vēra puella tuōs: Sīve cupis iuvenem, iuvenēs tibi mīlle placēbunt. Cōgēris vōtī nescius esse tuī: Seu tē forte iuvat sēra et sapientior aetās,        45 Hoc quoque, crēde mihī, plēnius agmen erit.





Sed tū praecipuē curvīs vēnāre theātrīs; Haec loca sunt vōtō fertiliōra tuō. Illīc inveniēs quod amēs, quod lūdere possīs, Quodque semel tangās, quodque tenēre velīs.        50 Ut redit itque frequēns longum formīca per agmen, Grāniferō solitum cum vehit ōre cibum, Aut ut apēs saltūsque suōs et olentia nactae Pāscua per flōrēs et thyma summa volant, Sīc ruit ad celebrēs cultissima fēmina lūdōs;        55 Copia iudicium saepe morata meum est .

Notes

l. 1: quis. After sī, quis has the meaning of aliquis, "anyone."

l. 2: carmen, carminis, neu. A poem (not the rare meaning "a card for wool").

l. 2: doctus is a secondary predication. It does not so much specify the subject as it modifies amet. It can be rendered something like "as a learned man."

l. 3: citae. Perfect Passive Participle of cieō. It means "speedy."

l. 3: vēlum, vēlī, neu. A sail.

l. 4: lĕvis, lĕve. With a short e in the stem, it means "light." With a long e, "smooth."

l. 5: Curribus ... habēnīs. The adjective aptus is often further qualified by a dative: "suitable for x."

l. 5: Automedōn, Automedontis, masc. Automedon. The name of the charioteer of the famous warrior Achilles.

l. 6: Tīphys, Tīphyos, masc. Tiphys. The name of the pilot of the famous ship Argo.

l. 6: Haemonius, -a, -um. Haemonia is an old word for Thessaly, where Argo came from. Translate "Haemonian."

l. 7: praefēcit. This verb means "put x in charge of y." The person put in charge is in the ACC; the matter one is put in chage of is in the DAT.

l. 7: artificem. A secondary predication: "as an expert."



l. 9: Phoebus, Phoebī, masc. Phoebus. A name for Apollo.

l. 9: datās. Equivalent to datās esse. What kind of infinitive clause?

l. 11: Clīō, Clīūs, fem. Clio. The Muse of history. The word follows a Greek declension pattern.

l. 12: Ascra, Ascrae, fem. Ascra. A town in the Greek province of Boeotia, birthplace of the poet Hesiod. Hesiod claimed that he met the Muses while he was tending his flocks. Ovid here addresses the town directly in a literary device known as apostrophe.

l. 13: pārēte. The verb pareō means "to be obedient to x" where x is DAT.

l. 14: ades. The verb adsum means "to be helpful to x: where x is DAT.

l. 15-16: vittae ... instita. These vocatives are another example of apostrophe. The vitta is a headband associated with married women. The instita is perhaps a narrow band sewn to the bottom of the stola. The stola was a long garment reserved at Rome for married women. Ovid is here using a complicated metonymy to convey the idea that married women, i.e., "respectable" women, should not read further. He doesn't want to appear a threat to anyone's marriage.

l. 17: Venerem. Venus means "sex." "Safe sex" here does not refer to health but to moral/legal consequences.

l. 17: furta. "Thefts" here means something like "stolen sexual encounters" in the sense of sex outside the usual rules of society and therfore kept secret. concessa furta, "permitted thefts," i.e., "permitted illicit sex" is an oxymoron, another literary/rhetorical figure.



l. 19: Principiō. ABL of point in time/space.

l. 19: quod ... velīs. A headless relative clause because there is no explicit antecedent. It is the OBJ of the matrix verb. Translate, "something you may love."

l. 19: velīs. The verb is subjunctive to indicate a potential rather than an specific lover. This use of a subjunctive in a relative clause is sometimes called a "relative clause of characteristic."

l. 20: mīles. A secondary predication. Translate "as a soldier" or the like.

l. 20: venīs. This is the verb of another headless relative clause. The clause functions as the SBJ of labōrā in the previous line.

l. 21: Proximus. Can be specified: "closest to x" where x is DAT.

l. 21: exōrāre. A third kind of infinitive clause: the subject infinitive. This structure only occurs when the matrix verb is sum or equivalent. English has a similar structure: "to know me is to love me."

l. 22: dūret. Ut + a subjunctive verb sometimes forms a substantive clause. We can often translate with a "that" clause.

l. 23: currū. Recall that Ovid considers himself the driver of the chariot of love.

l. 24: mēta. The turning posts or turning point in a Roman race track. Steering too close to the mēta was the cause of many wrecks.



1. 25: Ovid continues the image or horse racing, but now the reader seems to be the horse. What does it mean to have "loose reins" in that context?

l. 26: cui dīcās is a headless relative. This whole clause is the OBJ of Ēlige.

l. 28: oculīs. This can be taken as an instrumental ABL with quaerenda or as a DAT specifying apta.

l. 29: tendat. This verb can have a frame of "X sets [a trap] for Y," where the trap is ACC and Y is DAT.

l. 31: sustinet. A headless relative clause.

l. 32: multō. This adjective can have a plural meaning even when singular. Compare the English usage of "many" + a singluar noun: "many a fish."

l. 34: Ante. Here used as adverb rather than preposition. The word order is jumbled; see the associated tree.

l. 35: quaerentem. Here a "headless" participle, therefore a substantive use.

l. 36: tibi. Recall that DAT can indicate an agent argument which the passive periphrastic (FUT PASS PART + a form of sum).



l. 37: Gargăra, Gargărōrum, n. plur. Gargara. The name of a city near Mt. Ida and Troy. This sentence includes several ellipses. Pay close attention to the associated dependency tree.

l. 37: Mēthymna, Mēthymnae, fem. Methymna. The name of a city on the island of Lesbos.

l. 37: quot. An indeclinable plural adjective (indeclinable meaning it does not change its form for gender, number or case. The word means "how many" or "as many as."

l. 40: Aenēae. It is uncertain whether this word specifies mater or urbe. The ambiguity may be intentional.

l. 44: vōtī. "Prayer" or "wish"; here in the sense of "which lover to pray/wish for."

l. 47: theātrīs. ABL of place.

l. 48: vōtō. ABL or comparison.

l. 49-50: These lines contains four headless reletive clauses, each introduced by quod.

l. 51: Ut. Ut + Indicative is usually a comparison clause. Translate "as." This clause begins a simile. Roman poetry is famous for using this figure of speech in abundance.
