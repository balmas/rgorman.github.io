xx

Ars Amatoria



Ovid proves his claim that the theater is a good place to find a mate by offering an ezample from Rome in the distant past.









Spectātum veniunt, veniunt spectentur ut ipsae: Ille locus castī damna pudōris habet. Prīmus sollicitōs fēcistī, Rōmule, lūdōs, Cum iūvit viduōs rapta Sabīna virōs.        60 Tunc neque marmoreō pendēbant vēla theātrō, Nec fuerant liquidō pulpita rubra crocō; Illīc quās tulerant nemorōsa Palātia, frondēs Simpliciter positae, scēna sine arte fuit; In gradibus sēdit populus dē caespite factīs,        65 Quālibet hirsūtās fronde tegente comās. Respiciunt, oculīsque notant sibi quisque puellam Quam velit, et tacitō pectore multa movent. Dumque, rudem praebente modum tībīcine Tuscō, Lūdius aequātam ter pede pulsat humum,        70 In mediō plausū (plausus tunc arte cārēbant) Rēx populō praedae signa petīta dēdit. Prōtinus exiliunt, animum clāmōre fatentēs, Virginibus cupidās iniciuntque manūs. Ut fugiunt aquilās, timidissima turba, columbae,        75 Ut fugit invīsōs agna novella lupōs: Sīc illae timuēre virōs sine mōre ruentēs; Cōnstitit in nūllā quī fuit ante color. Nam timor ūnus erat, faciēs nōn ūna timōris: Pars laniat crīnēs, pars sine mente sedet;        80 Altera maesta silet, frūstrā vocat altera mātrem: Haec queritur, stupet haec; haec manet, illa fugit; Dūcuntur raptae, geniālis praeda, puellae, Et potuit multās ipse decēre timor. Sī qua repugnārat nimium comitemque negābat,        85 Sublātam cupīdō vir tulit ipse sinū, Atque ita 'quid tenerōs lacrimīs corrumpis ocellōs? Quod mātrī pater est, hoc tibi' dīxit 'erō.' Rōmule, mīlitibus scīstī dare commoda sōlus: Haec mihi sī dederīs commoda, mīles erō.        90 Scīlicet ex illō sollemnia mōre theātra Nunc quoque fōrmōsīs īnsidiōsa manent.







Nec tē nōbilium fugiat certāmen equōrum; Multa capāx populī commoda Circus habet. Nīl opus est digitīs, per quōs arcāna loquāris,        95 Nec tibi per nūtūs accipienda nota est: Proximus ā dominā, nūllō prohibente, sedētō, Iunge tuum laterī quā potes usque latus; Et bene, quod cōgit, sī nōlīs, līnea iungī, Quod tibi tangenda est lēge puella locī.        100 Hīc tibi quaerātur sociī sermōnis orīgō, Et moveant prīmōs pūblica verba sonōs. Cuius equī veniant, facitō, studiōse, requīrās: Nec mora, quisquis erit, cui favet illa, favē. At cum pompa frequēns caelestibus ībit eburnīs,        105 Tū Venerī dominae plaude favente manū; Utque fit, in gremium pulvis sī forte puellae Dēciderit, digitīs excutiendus erit: Etsī nūllus erit pulvis, tamen excute nūllum: Quaelibet officiō causa sit apta tuō.        110 Pallia sī terra nimium dēmissa iacēbunt, Collige, et inmundā sēdulus effer humō; Prōtinus, officiī pretium, patiente puellā Contingent oculīs crūra videnda tuīs. Respice praetereā, post vōs quīcumque sedēbit,        115 Nē premat oppositō mollia terga genū. Parva levēs capiunt animōs: fuit ūtile multīs Pulvīnum facilī composuisse manū. Prōfuit et tenuī ventōs mōvisse tabellā, Et cava sub tenerum scamna dedisse pedem.        120 Hōs aditūs Circusque novō praebēbit amōrī, Sparsaque sollicitō trīstis harēna forō. Illā saepe puer Veneris pugnāvit harēnā, Et quī spectāvit vulnera, vulnus habet. Dum loquitur tangitque manum poscitque libellum        125 Et quaerit positō pignore, vincat uter, Saucius ingemuit tēlumque volātile sēnsit, Et pars spectātī mūneris ipse fuit. Quid, modo cum bellī nāvālis imāgine Caesar Persīdās indūxit Cēcropiāsque ratēs?          130 Nempe ab utrōque marī iuvenēs, ab utrōque puellae Vēnēre, atque ingēns orbis in Urbe fuit. Quis nōn invēnit turbā, quod amāret, in illā? Ēheu, quam multōs advena torsit amor! Dant etiam positīs aditum convīvia mēnsīs:        135 Est aliquid praeter vīna, quod inde petās. Vīna parant animōs faciuntque calōribus aptōs: Cūra fugit multō dīluiturque merō. Tunc veniunt rīsūs, tum pauper cornua sūmit, Tum dolor et cūrae rūgaque frontis abit.          140 Tunc aperit mentēs aevō rārissima nostrō Simplicitās, artēs excutiente deō. Illīc saepe animōs iuvenum rapuēre puellae, Et Venus in vīnīs ignis in igne fuit. Hīc tū fallācī nimium nē crēde lucernae:          145 Iūdiciō fōrmae noxque merumque nocent. Lūce deās caelōque Paris spectāvit apertō, Cum dīxit Venerī 'vincis utramque, Venus.' Nocte latent mendae, vitiōque ignōscitur omnī, Horaque fōrmōsam quamlibet illa facit.          150 Cōnsule dē gemmīs, dē tīnctā mūrice lānā, Cōnsule dē faciē corporibusque diem. Hāctenus, unde lēgās quod amēs, ubi rētia pōnās, Praecipit imparibus vecta Thalēa rotīs. Nunc tibi, quae placuit, quās sit capienda per artēs,          155 Dīcere praecipuae mōlior artis opus. Quisquis ubīque, virī, docilēs advertite mentēs, Pollicitīsque favēns vulgus, adeste meīs. Prīma tuae mentī veniat fīdūcia, cūnctās Posse capī; capiēs, tū modo tende plāgās.          160 Vēre prius volucrēs taceant, aestāte cicādae, Maenalius lepōrī det sua terga canis, Fēmina quam iuvenī blandē temptāta repugnet: Haec quoque, quam poteris crēdere nōlle, volet. Utque virō fūrtīva vēnus, sīc grāta puellae:          165 Vir male dissimulat: tēctius illa cupit. Conveniat maribus, nē quam nōs ante rogēmus, Fēmina iam partēs victa rogantīs agat. Mollibus in prātīs admūgit fēmina taurō: Fēmina cornipedī semper adhinnit equō.          170 Parcior in nōbīs nec tam furiōsa libīdō: Lēgitimum fīnem flamma virīlis habet. Byblida quid referam, vetitō quae frātris amōre Ārsit et est laqueō fortiter ulta nefās? Myrrha patrem, sed nōn quā fīlia dēbet, amāvit,          175 Et nunc obductō cortice pressa latet: Illius lacrimīs, quās arbore fundit odōrā, Unguimur, et dominae nōmina gutta tenet. Forte sub umbrōsīs nemorōsae vallibus Īdae Candidus, armentī glōria, taurus erat,          180 Signātus tenuī media inter cornua nigrō: Ūna fuit lābēs, cētera lactis erant. Illum Cnōsiadēsque Cydōnēaeque iuvencae Optārunt tergō sustinuisse suō. Pasiphaë fierī gaudēbat adultera taurī;          185 Invida fōrmōsās ōderat illa bovēs. Nōta canō: nōn hoc, centum quae sustinet urbēs, Quamvīs sit mendāx, Crēta negāre potest.

Notes





l. 57: Spectātum. This form of the verb is called a supine. It is the identical to the Perf Part Sing Neut Acc, where the Perf Part exists. It is used as an ADV to express purpose with verbs indicating motion. Translate "to observe."

l. 59: Prīmus. A secondary predication. The construction is very common with ordinals. Often translated "first to X" where X is the associated verb.

l. 59: sollicitōs. An object complement (OCOMP). The verb facio often takes the frame "make X Y" where X is the direct object and Y is an adjective or noun indicating a change in X. For example, in "you make me tired," me is the direct object and tired the object complement.

l. 62: crocō. An ABL noun can be used to specify or limit an adjective (as an adverbial ADV). Here, for example, "red with saffron."

l. 63: Palātia. The Palatine was the area of Rome where Augustus made his home. The plural Palātia may here indicate "Palatine areas."

l. 64: scēna. The SBJ of fuit. sine arte is dependent on scēna and functions as ATR.

l. 65: dē. Here with its occasional meaning "from."

l. 66: Quālibet. FEM SING ABL of quī-lĭbet (-lŭbet), quae-libet, quod-libet. This strange compound adjective is made from the relative adjective quī etc. and the verb libet "be pleasing." It has the force of an emphatic indefinate. It means something like "whoever / whichever you like."

l. 67: quisque. A SING NOM although the parent verb notant is PLUR. It is best to interpret as a secondary predication rather than SBJ. Interpret something like "They, each one, designate ..."

l. 68: movent. This verb often is used to indicate the presence of emotions. Thus, "they stir up many [emotions]" or, more colloquially, "they rile themselves up."

l. 69: Tuscō. From Tuscus, Tusca, Tuscum, "Tuscan" or "Etruscan." Tuscany is a region of Italy north of Rome.

l. 71: mediō. Medius can be used in two ways. It can indicate that its head noun is in the middle of something else or it can indicate the middle part of the head noun. This second use is called the partitive use of the adjective.

l. 71: plausus. Ovid may be referring a practice of his own time, when groups were hired to applaud at the theaters and law courts. Such a group is called a claque.

l. 74: iniciunt. This verb means to "put/throw X upon Y" where X is ACC and Y is DAT.

l. 76: invīsōs. The adjective invīsus, -a, -um can mean "hostile" or "hated."

l. 83: raptae. Take as secondary predication and translate "the girls are led abducted."

l. 84: decēre. decet can mean "to make X appear attractive, where X is ACC." Ovid is indeed saying that the signs of fear the women were sowing made them more attractive to the Romans.

l. 85: qua. FEM SING NOM meaning "any." This form of the adjective occurs after si.

l. 85: repugnārat. This is a syncopated form of the PLUPERF repugnāverat.

l. 86: Sublātam. PPP (PERFECT PASS PART) of tollō, sustulī, sublātus. The puella has been lifted by her kidnapper.

l. 86: sinū. sinus literally means a surface with a bend or fold. Here it refers to the man's arms wrapped around his captive.

l. 87: quid. Here NEU SING ACC used as ADV with meaning "why." The material in quotation marks is dependent (as asn OBJ) on dīxit.

l. 89: scīstī. With an infinitive OBJ rather than an indirect question, this verb means "know how to X" where X is the infinitive.

l. 89: commoda. Here with the meaning "benefits" as in our expression "fringe benefits."

l. 90: sī. As commonly in poetry, sī has been dispaced to the middle of its clause. The subordination indicated by sī in fact begins at the start of the line.

l. 91: Scīlicet. The word is here a sentence adverbial (AuxY) and means something like "obviously," or "evidentially."

l. 91: ex illō. Editors suggest that the phrase is short for ex illō tempore and means "from that time on."

Ovid turns his attention from the thaeater to the racetrack.

l. 93: fugiat. What is the most common meaning for a PRES SUBJ verb in a main clause?

l. 94: capāx. MASC SING NOM adjective meaning "able to hold a large quantity." The information, "a large quantity of what?" is often specified by a dependent GEN. In this use, the GEN is considered ADV because it limits or clarifies the meaning of an adjective.

l. 95: Nīl. A NEU SING ACC noun. The ACC is often adverbial meaning something like "to no extent" or "not at all."

l. 95: opus est. This is probably best taken as a so-called "light verb" phrase. In such expressions, usually composed of a verb and a noun, the inherent meaning of the verb loses its importance, and the verb serves primarily to add grammatical information such as tense. An English example is "he took a nap" for "he napped." The phrase opus est means "there is need for X" where X is ABL.

l. 97: Proximus. A secondary predication with sedētō.

l. 99: bene: Short for bene est, "It is well."

l. 99: quod. A subordinating conjunction introducing a substantive "that clause."

l. 100: Quod. Another substantive clause coordinated with the quod-clause in the previous line.

l. 102: pūblica. pūblica verba are "public words" in the sense of conversations that anyone could listen to without scandal.

l. 103: veniant. What kind of clause: Qu- word, SUBJ verb, dependant of verb meaning "ask"?

l. 103: requīrās. Dependant on facio we often find a SBJ (with or wihout ut) in a construction sometimes called a Substantive Clause of Result. In such passages, facio means "ensure/accompish X" where X is the substantive clause.

l. 104: mora. Short for mora sit. What is the most likely meaning for a PRES SUBJ in a main clause?

l. 104: erit. The verb of a headless relative clause that serves as the OBJ of favē.

l. 105: eburnīs. The adjective frequēns is sometimes further specified with a dependent ABL, in the sense of "crowded with X" where X is ABL. The reference is to a pre-race procession, in which statues of the gods were carried through the arena.

l. 107: sī. As happens in poetry, this subordinating conjunction has been delayed to the middle of the clause.

l. 110: Quaelibet. An adjective in the FEM SING NOM, meaning "any at all."

l. 110: officiō. The relevant definition here is "A voluntary service, a kindness, favor, courtesy, rendered to one whose claim to it is recognized." Note that officium can be contrasted with beneficium, "a service rendered where there is no claim." Thus, the use of officium implies that the man here does a "service" for the woman with the added nuance that the woman deserves to be served in this way.

l. 111: dēmissa. The word often means "low," or "low-hanging" when apples to clothing, etc.

l. 113. pretium. Here means "reward."

l. 114: crūra videnda. A strange and rare use of the participle, called the "dominant participle construction." Simply put, the words do not have the expected meaning of Noun + Attribute ("shins to be seen") but rather something like "the seeing of [her] shins," or "seeing [her] shins."

l. 115: sedēbit. The verb of a headless relative clause. The clause is here best taken as the SBJ of premat.

l. 118: composuisse. An infinitive can be the SBJ of tenses of est.

l. 119: Prōfuit. This verb, meaning "be advantageous," often has an infinitive clause as its SBJ: "X is advantageous" where X is an infinitive and dependents. We can translate "it was advantageous to ..."

l. 119: ventōs. Here can be translated "breezes."

l. 119: tabellā. A "little board" is a tablet, here perhaps used to keep track of teams, wagers, etc.
