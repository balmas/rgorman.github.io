xx

Ars Amatoria



Ovid proves his claim that the theater is a good place to find a mate by offering an ezample from Rome in the distant past.









Spectātum veniunt, veniunt spectentur ut ipsae: Ille locus castī damna pudōris habet. Prīmus sollicitōs fēcistī, Rōmule, lūdōs, Cum iūvit viduōs rapta Sabīna virōs.        60 Tunc neque marmoreō pendēbant vēla theātrō, Nec fuerant liquidō pulpita rubra crocō; Illīc quās tulerant nemorōsa Palātia, frondēs Simpliciter positae, scēna sine arte fuit; In gradibus sēdit populus dē caespite factīs,        65 Quālibet hirsūtās fronde tegente comās. Respiciunt, oculīsque notant sibi quisque puellam Quam velit, et tacitō pectore multa movent. Dumque, rudem praebente modum tībīcine Tuscō, Lūdius aequātam ter pede pulsat humum,        70 In mediō plausū (plausus tunc arte cārēbant) Rēx populō praedae signa petīta dēdit. Prōtinus exiliunt, animum clāmōre fatentēs, Virginibus cupidās iniciuntque manūs. Ut fugiunt aquilās, timidissima turba, columbae,        75 Ut fugit invīsōs agna novella lupōs: Sīc illae timuēre virōs sine mōre ruentēs; Cōnstitit in nūllā quī fuit ante color. Nam timor ūnus erat, faciēs nōn ūna timōris: Pars laniat crīnēs, pars sine mente sedet;        80 Altera maesta silet, frūstrā vocat altera mātrem: Haec queritur, stupet haec; haec manet, illa fūgit; Dūcuntur raptae, geniālis praeda, puellae, Et potuit multās ipse decēre timor. Sī quā repugnārat nimium comitemque negābat,        85 Sublātam cupīdō vir tulit ipse sinū, Atque ita 'quid tenerōs lacrimīs corrumpis ocellōs? Quod mātrī pater est, hoc tibi' dīxit 'erō.' Rōmule, mīlitibus scīstī dare commoda sōlus: Haec mihi sī dederīs commoda, mīles erō.        90 Scīlicet ex illō sollemnia mōre theātra Nunc quoque fōrmōsīs īnsidiōsa manent. Nec tē nōbilium fugiat certāmen equōrum; Multa capāx populī commoda Circus habet. Nīl opus est digitīs, per quōs arcāna loquāris, Nec tibi per nūtūs accipienda nota est: Proximus ā dominā, nūllō prohibente, sedētō, Iunge tuum laterī quā potes usque latus; Et bene, quod cōgit, sī nōlīs, līnea iungī, Quod tibi tangenda est lēge puella locī. Hīc tibi quaerātur sociī sermōnis orīgō, Et moveant prīmōs pūblica verba sonōs. Cuius equī veniant, facitō, studiōse, requīrās: Nec mora, quisquis erit, cui favet illa, favē.

Notes





l. 57: Spectātum. This form of the verb is called a supine. It is the identical to the Perf Part Sing Neut Acc, where the Perf Part exists. It is used as an ADV to express purpose with verbs indicating motion. Translate "to observe."

l. 59: Prīmus. A secondary predication. The construction is very common with ordinals. Often translated "first to X" where X is the associated verb.

l. 59: sollicitōs. An object complement (OCOMP). The verb facio often takes the frame "make X Y" where X is the direct object and Y is an adjective or noun indicating a change in X. For example, in "you make me tired," me is the direct object and tired the object complement.

l. 62: crocō. An ABL noun can be used to specify or limit an adjective (as an adverbial ADV). Here, for example, "red with saffron."

l. 63: Palātia. The Palatine was the area of Rome where Augustus made his home. The plural Palātia may here indicate "Palatine areas."

l. 64: scēna. The SBJ of fuit. sine arte is dependent on scēna and functions as ATR.

l. 65: dē. Here with its occasional meaning "from."

l. 66: Quālibet. FEM SING ABL of quī-lĭbet (-lŭbet), quae-libet, quod-libet. This strange compound adjective is made from the relative adjective quī etc. and the verb libet "be pleasing." It has the force of an emphatic indefinate. It means something like "whoever / whichever you like."

l. 67: quisque. A SING NOM although the parent verb notant is PLUR. It is best to interpret as a ssecondary predication rather than SBJ. Interpret something like "They, each one, designate ..."

l. 68: movent. This verb often is used to indicate the presence of emotions. Thus, "they stir up many [emotions]" or, more colloquially, "they rile themselves up."

l. 69: Tuscō. From Tuscus, Tusca, Tuscum, "Tuscan" or "Etruscan." Tuscany is a region of Italy north of Rome.

l. 71: plausus. Ovid may be referring a practice of his own time, when groups were hired to applaud at the theaters and law courts. Such a group is called a claque.
