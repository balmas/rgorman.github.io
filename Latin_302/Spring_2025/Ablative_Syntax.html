Ablative Case Semantics

Separation & Origin

The ablative case frequently expresses separation, referring to the place whence or the concept of origin. These modifiers are typically constructed using a(b), dē, e(x), sine + ablative, though prepositions are omitted when referring to towns and small islands—particularly in poetry.

Eō ex finitimīs populīs turba omnis avidō novārum rērum perfugit.
"A whole crowd of people from neighboring tribes, eager for a new life, took refuge." (Livy 1.8.6)

Distance & Measurement

The ablative case can also indicate distance, and in such cases, no preposition is required.

[Horātius] videt [hostēs] magnīs intervallīs sequentēs.
"Horatius saw his enemies follow at great intervals." (Livy 1.25.8)

Ablative of Agent

For animate beings performing an action in the passive voice, the ablative of agent is used with a(b) + ablative.

Volturcius a legatis desertus est.
"Volturcius was abandoned by the envoys." (Sall. Cat. 45.4)

Cause or Reason

This construction expresses either a factual reason for an event or an argument given for an action or state.

Plurimus hic aeger moritur vigilando.
"Here many a sick man dies from lack of sleep." (Juv. Sat. 3.232)

Comparison (Ablative of Comparison)

Used without quam, this form expresses a "point from which" a comparison is viewed.

Luce sunt clariora nobis tua consilia omnia!
"Clearer than the sunlight are all your plans for us!" (Cic. Cat. 1.6)

Ablative of Place Where

Typically constructed using in, sub, prō or without a preposition for towns and small islands.

Erat Athēnis spatiosa domus, sed infāmis.
"Once upon a time, there was in Athens a spacious but infamous house." (Pliny 7.27.5)

Ablative of Time When/Within Which

Usually constructed without a preposition unless emphasizing duration.

Quid in bellō [possunt]?
"What are they capable of in times of war?" (Caes. B.G. 2.4.1)

Ablative of Means

This construction expresses the instrument or tool used to perform an action and is generally without a preposition.

Tum [Daedalus pennās] līnō mediās et cērīs alligat īmās.
"Then Daedalus bound the feathers together in the middle with flaxen thread and at the bottom with wax." (Ovid Met. 8.193)

Ablative of Accompaniment

Formed using cum + ablative, indicating "together with" or "nearby."

Desinant obsidēre cum gladiīs cīvium.
"Let them stop besieging the senate building armed with swords." (Cic. Cat. 1.32)

Ablative of Manner

May be constructed with or without cum + ablative. The noun may or may not be modified by an attribute.

Incredibilī celeritāte ad flūmen dēcurrērunt.
"They ran down to the river at incredible speed." (Caes. B.G. 2.19.7)

Ablative of Route

Expressed without a preposition when indicating a road, bridge, or path.

Aureliā Vīā profectus est.
"He left by the Aurelian Way." (Cic. Cat. 2.6)

Ablative of Price

Appears without a preposition to express monetary value.

Emī virginem trīgintā mīnīs.
"I bought a young woman for thirty minae." (Plautus Curc. 343–44)

Descriptive Ablative

Acts either as an attribute of a noun or part of the predicate with a copula.

Catilīna fuit magnā vī et animī et corporis.
"Catiline was very strong in mind and body." (Sall. Cat. 5.1)
