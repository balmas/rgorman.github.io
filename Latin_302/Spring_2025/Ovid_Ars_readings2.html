More Selections from Ovid's Ars Amatoria

More about the theater: refer to treebank, #39-40

1

Spectātum veniunt, veniunt spectentur ut ipsae:

Ille locus castī damna pudōris habet.

  • Spectātum: a poorly understood grammatical structure called the (1st) supine. This "supine" is identical to the mas/neu acc of the perfect participle. However, it is active rather than passive and is generally used to indicate purpose with verbs indicating motion. Here = "to observe (others)"
  • spectentur ut: remember that in poetry a subordinating conjunction may be displaced from the beginning of the clause
  • ut: + subjunctive → a "that/so that" clause
  • damna .. habet: = "has damage of ..." = "is harmful to ..."

Here we omit Ovid's retelling of the story of the Sabine Women. Ovid now summarizes the lasting effects of the ancient events (refer to treebank # 60):

2

Scīlicet ex illō sollemnia mōre theātra

Nunc quoque fōrmōsīs īnsidiōsa manent.

  • sollemnia: in the Roman and Greek world, dramatic performances (and the theater in which they were held) were often associated with the gods and religion
  • mōre: = "tradition"
  • fōrmōsīs: substantized adjective, = "the beautiful"; DAT(X) = for X/for the advantage/disadvantage of X"
  • īnsidiōsa: = "unexpectedly dangerous"

The next recommended place to find "romance" is the Circus (not the one with clowns!) (Refer to treebank # 61-78)

3

Nec tē nōbilium fugiat certāmen equōrum;

Multa capāx populī commoda Circus habet.

  • fugiat: present subjunctive in main clause → volitive; volitive = "an expression of will" = a command, request, plea, etc. English formula = "let the SBJ verb"
  • capāx: = "fit for X" (in the sense of being spacious enough for X), where X us genitive
  • commoda: = "advantageous things" = "advantages"
  • Circus: PLN, the Circus Maximus, a chariot-racing stadium said by some to be large enough for 250,000 spectators.

Ovid now explains some of these advantages to looking for a lover at the Circus

4

Nīl opus est digitīs, per quōs arcāna loquāris, 95

Nec tibi per nūtūs accipienda nota est:

  • Nīl: = nihil; this noun is indeclinable, i.e., it has only one form for all combinations of number and case. In poetry, neuter accusative adjectives often function as adverbials; = "to the extent of nothing" = "not at all"
  • opus est: this combination is best treated as equivalent to a verb: = "there is need of/work for X" where X is ABL
  • loquāris: in relative clauses, the subjunctive verb often indicates a potential (rather than an actual) action/event; this mood serves to generalize; = "may/might communicate"
  • tibi: "by you"; a meaning of the DAT that is common as a dependency of a passive periphrastic verb
  • nota: = "a sign"
  • accipienda est: debitive aspect = SBJ must be + -EN(VERB)
5

Proximus ā dominā, nūllō prohibente, sedētō,

Iunge tuum laterī quā potes usque latus;

  • Proximus ā dominā ...: In the Circus, unlike the theater, there were no strict rules about seating arrangement. Thus women and men could sit together.
  • Proximus: in Latin the expression is "close/closest from X" rather than the English "to X"; proximus here functions as a secondary predication
  • ā: prep. + ABL(X) = "from X"
  • dominā: can be a term of endearment
  • prohibente: Ablative Absolute construction; translation formula = "with SBJ(X) + -ING(VERB)" where X is ABL
  • sedētō: the so-called Future Imperative has an archaic and solemn sense. Cf. the biblical "Thou shalt ..."
  • Iunge: verb frame = SBJ touches X to Y, where X is ACC and Y is DAT
  • quā: a relative adverb introducing an autonymous relative clause; = "where"
  • usque: best translated as a dependency of quā: = "wherever"
6

Et bene , quod cōgit, sī nōlīs, līnea iungī,

Quod tibi tangenda est lēge puella locī. 100

  • bene: ellipsed form of bene est; the ellipsed est needs a SBJ and it is best to assume that this element is provided by the quod-clauses
  • quod cōgit: quod here is a subordinating conjunction rather than a relative pronoun. It can be translated "that the SBJ compels ..." The quod-clause as a whole is the SBJ of bene est.
  • cōgit: verb fram= SBJ compels Y, where Y is a complementary infinitive clause.
  • nōlīs: subjunctive mood = potential event/action
  • līnea: a chalk line on the bench separated individual "seats"
  • iungī: the SBJ of this clause is omitted and must be "restored" anaphorically
  • Quod: another "that-clause" with quod acting as a subordinating conjunction
  • tibi: = "by you/by your agency"
  • tangenda est: present debitive passive = SBJ must be -EN(VERB)
  • lēge: ABL(X) without prep. = from/from the cause of/due to/because of X"
7

Hīc tibi quaerātur sociī sermōnis orīgō,

Et moveant prīmōs pūblica verba sonōs.

  • Hīc: = "here"
  • tibi: DAT(X) can = "by you/by your agency"
  • quaerātur: present subjunctive → present volitive; = "let + BASE(VERB); here the formula for the English passive must also be added
  • sociī: = "friendly" in contrast to "romantic" or "intimate"
  • moveant: present subjunctive → present volitive; = "let + BASE(VERB)
  • pūblica: = something like "suitable for the public" in contrast to "private"
  • verba: Ovid may be implying that words as formed in the mind give rise to the sounds associated with these words
8

Cuius equī veniant , facitō, studiōse, requīrās :

Nec mora , quisquis erit , cui favet illa, favē.

  • This couplet gives an example of how to start a socius sermo
  • Cuius: an interrogative pronoun
  • veniant: what kind of clause is this: interrogative word, subjunctive verb, matrix verb of information processing?
  • facitō: = "do" in the English emphatic sense, e.g, "do tell" or "do go on" etc. The verb frame is [SBJ] do X, where X is a subjunctive clause. The use of the so-called future imperative archaic and solemn.
  • studiōse: = "eager (person)" = "student"
  • requīrās: = "ask"
  • mora: apparently the SBJ of an ellipsed verb sit meaning "let there be"
  • quisquis erit: autonymous relative clause; promoted to the role of its putative antecedent and functions as OBJ of favē
  • cui favet illa: relative clause; either attributive directly dependent on quisquis or, more probably, autonomous and functioning as the predicate nominal of erit
  • favet: verb frame = SBJ supports/favors X, where X is DAT
9

At cum pompa frequēns caelestibus ībit eburnīs , 105

Tū Venerī dominae plaude favente manū;

  • cum: subordinating conjunction, not prep. = "when"
  • pompa: a parade held before the racing
  • frequēns: = "crowded with X" where X is ABL without a prep.
  • plaude: verb frame = SBJ applauds for X, where X is DAT
  • manū: ABL(X) without prep. = "with/by means of X"
10

Utque fit, in gremium pulvis sī forte puellae

Dēciderit, digitīs excutiendus erit:

  • Ut: subordinating conjuction = "as"
  • fit: = "happens"; chariot racing was a dusty business
  • gremium: = "lap"
  • sī: as often in poetry, the subordinating conjunction is shifted from its expected place at the beginning of its clause
  • Dēciderit: = "fall down"; future perfect → completed future = "will have + -EN(VERB); this tense construction can be translated with the English present tense; the use of the future perfect emphasizes the high probability of the event: dust will fall
  • digitīs: ABL(X) without prep. = "with/by means of X"
  • excutiendus erit: → debitive future passive = SBJ will have to be/must be + -EN(VERB)
11

Etsī nūllus erit pulvis, tamen excute nūllum:

Quaelibet officiō causa sit apta tuō. 110

  • nūllum: =the non-existent (dust)"
  • Quaelibet: quīlibet, quaelibet, quodlibet are so-called "free-choice adjectives" = "whoever/whatever/whichever you please"
  • officiō: = "kind service"
  • sit: present subjunctive → present volitive; = "let SBJ BASE(VERB)"
  • apta: = "suitable for X" where X is DAT
12

Pallia sī terrā nimium dēmissa iacēbunt,

Collige, et inmundā sēdulus effer humō;

  • Pallia: = "cloaks"; the plural may refer to the repetition of this action on different occassions
  • terrā: ABL(X) without prep. = "at/in/on X"
  • dēmissa: = "hanging down"
  • Collige: verb frame = "SBJ picks up X" where X is ACC; here X must be understood anaphorically
  • sēdulus: a secondary predication
  • humō: ABL(X) without prep. = "from X"
13

Prōtinus, officiī pretium, patiente puellā

Contingent oculīs crūra videnda tuīs.

  • Prōtinus: = "immediately"
  • officiī: = "kind service"
  • pretium: "reward"; pretium is in apposition to the main clause; the event related in the main clause is therefore equated with/described as an officiī pretium
  • patiente puellā: Ablative Absolute clause; use the formula "with SBJ + -ING(VERB)
  • Contingent oculīs crūra videnda tuīs: one of the more oddly expressed clauses in Ovid
  • Contingent: verb frame = "SBJ fortunately happens to/befalls X," where X is DAT
  • crūra videnda: literally = "legs being seen"; better English would be "a view of her legs"
14

Respice praetereā, post vōs quīcumque sedēbit, 115

Nē premat oppositō mollia terga genū.

  • Respice: verb frame = "SBJ takes care that X" where X is an ut/nē + subjunctive clause
  • post vōs quīcumque sedēbit: autonomous relative clause, promoted to function of its putative antecedent, thus SBJ of premat
  • post: prep. + ACC(X) = "behind/after X"
  • quīcumque: quīcumque, quaecumque, quodcumque, another so-called "free choice adjective/pronoun" = "whoever/whatever/whichever
  • oppositō: = "stuck out/placed in the way
  • terga: again we would expect a singular, but Ovid is describing a general strategy that should be repeated as often as is useful
15
Parva levēs capiunt animōs: fuit ūtile multīs

Pulvīnum facilī composuisse manū.

  • Parva: = "small things"; an attributive adjective non directly dependent on a "noun" takes on the syntactic function of that "noun"
  • levēs: = "light" = "non-serious"; at entertainments such as the races, peoples' spirits may be unencumbered by serious matters
  • fuit ...: an example of a "small thing" that may impress a potential lover
  • Pulvīnum: = "seat cushion"
  • composuisse: = "arrange"; infinitives can often function as the SBJ or PNOM of est; in poetry, the perfect infinitive can be used as the apparent equivalent of the present infinitive
  • facilī: lit. = "easy"; here combines the senses of "friendly" and "dexterous"
  • manū: ABL(X) without prep. = "with/by means of X"
16

Prōfuit et tenuī ventōs mōvisse tabellā,

Et cava sub tenerum scamna dedisse pedem. 120

  • Prōfuit: verb frame = "SBJ(X) is useful/advantageous" where X is a infinitive clause
  • et ... Et: "both ... and"
  • ventōs: = "winds" = "breezes"; again, the plural may represent the repetition of this strategy on many occasions
  • mōvisse: probably equivalent to present infinitive
  • tabellā: = "racing form/program"; ABL(X) without prep. = with/by means of X
  • cava: the meaning of this attribute is unclear; perhaps scamna were "hollow" to reduce weight for portability
  • sub: prep + ACC(X) = "beneath/under X"
  • scamna: = "foot stools"; on the plural see ventōs, above
  • dedisse: = "provide"; in the tense, see mōvisse, above
17

Hōs aditūs Circusque novō praebēbit amōrī,

Sparsaque sollicitō trīstis harēna forō.

  • aditūs: = "approaches/opportunities"
  • Circusque: -que ... -que = "both ... and"; here two SBJs of the verb are conjoined
  • praebēbit: verb frame = "SBJ offers/presents ACC(X) to DAT(Y)
  • Sparsa: = "sprinkled" (PERF PART of spargō, spargere, spārsī)
  • trīstis: = "sad/sorrowful" because of the associated violence and death
  • harēna: = "sand"; often used to refer to the location of gladiatorial combat
  • forō: ABL(X) wihtout prep. = "at/in/on X"; gladiatorial combats were often held in temporary arenas set up in a market place
18

Illā saepe puer Veneris pugnāvit harēnā,

Et quī spectāvit vulnera, vulnus habet.

  • harēnā: ABL(X) without prep. = "at/in/on X"
  • quī spectāvit vulnera: autonomous relative clause, promoted to syntactic role of its "antecedent"
  • vulnera: literal = "wounds" of the gladiators
  • vulnus: figurative = "a wound" inflicted by the puer Veneris
19

Dum loquitur tangitque manum poscitque libellum 125

Et quaerit positō pignore, vincat uter,

Saucius ingemuit tēlumque volātile sēnsit,

Et pars spectātī mūneris ipse fuit.

  • Dum: subordinating conjunction = "while"
  • loquitur: verb frame = "SBJ converses/talks"
  • manum: here = "(her) hand";
  • poscit: verb frame = "SBJ asks for ACC(X)"
  • libellum: = "program/list of fighters"
  • quaerit: = verb frame = "SBJ asks INDIR_QUEST(X)" where X meets the criteria of the IQ clause
  • positō: ABL ABS; use the formula "with SBJ[ABL(X)] having been + -EN(VERB)"
  • pignore: = "a bet/wager"
  • uter: an interrogative adjective/pronoun, = "which of two/the two"
  • Saucius: secondary predication
  • tēlum: a general term applying to a projectile weapon, such as an arrow or a spear
  • volātile: = "flying"
  • mūneris: lit. = "a public service", often = "a gladiatorial show+ put on by a public official
  • spectātī mūneris: lit. = "watched service" = "the show he had watched" or the like
20

Quid, modo cum bellī nāvālis imāgine Caesar

Persīdās indūxit Cēcropiāsque ratēs? 130

  • Quid: often used emphatically, to draw attention to a statement; = "what about that time ... ?" or the like
  • modo: = "recently"; the event referred to occurred in 2 BCE
  • cum: subordinating conjunction = "when"
  • imāgine: = "imitation/representation"; ABL(X) without prep. = "because of/due to X"
  • Caesar: Augustus
  • Persīdās: = "Persian"
  • Cēcropiās: Cēcropius, Cēcropia, Cēcropium, = "pertaining to/connected with Cecrops," the mythical first king of Athens; = "Athenian"
