Selections from Ovid's Ars Amatoria

1

Sī quis in hōc artem populō nōn nōvit amandī,

Hoc legat et lectō carmine doctus amet.

  • quis: = "anyone"
  • amandī: gerund, sing. neut. gen. = "loving"
  • lectō carmine: best as ABL absolute
  • doctus: a secondary predication; this structure is often best rendered into English with "as"
2

Arte citae vēlōque ratēs rēmōque moventur,

Arte levēs currūs: arte regendus amor.

  • vēlō: vēlum, vēlī, neu. = "sail"
  • -que: conj., X-que = "and X"
  • levēs: the two adjectives levis, -e, and lēvis, -e are easily confused. lev- with a short e in the stem = light; lēv- with a long e = "smooth"
  • currūs: = "chariots"
  • regendus: gerundive, also called future passive participle. Here regendus is short for regendus est. This combiniation is often called the "passive periphrastic construction." It is always debitive and passive = SBJ must be verbed.
3

Curribus Automedōn lentīsque erat aptus habēnīs, 5

Tīphys in Haemoniā puppe magister erat:

  • Automedōn, Automedōn Automedontis, mas. PrN (proper name); the charioteer of Achilles
  • aptus: aptus, apta, aptum = "adept." Often + DAT (= "adept at DAT")
  • Tīphys, sing. mas. nom., PrN; the pilot of the Argo
  • Haemoniā, Haemonius, Haemonia, Haemonium = "Thessalian." Thessaly is a PLN (proper name of a place)
4

Mē Venus artificem tenerō praefēcit Amōrī;

Tīphys et Automedōn dīcar Amōris ego.

  • artificem: = "expert." A secondary predication; translate "a an expert"
  • praefēcit: verb frame = SBJ puts X in charge or Y, where X is ACC and Y is DAT
  • Amōrī: = "love" here personified as the son of Venus = "Cupid"
  • dīcar: verb frame = SBJ is called X, where X is the same case as SBJ
5

Nōn ego , Phoebe , datās ā tē mihi mentiar artēs,

Nec nōs āëriae vōce monēmur avis, 10

Nec mihi sunt vīsae Clīō Clīūsque sorōrēs

Servantī pecudēs vallibus, Ascra, tuīs:

  • Phoebe: Phoebus, Phoebī, mas. A PrN referring to Apollo
  • datās: = datās esse = PERF PASS INFIN
  • ā: prep + ABL(X) = "through the agency of/by X"
  • mentiar: verb frame = SBJ falsely states X, where X is an indirect statement clause
  • avis: perhaps a recherché reference to prophecy. Zeus was said to have sent a dove to Dodona in Nothwest Greece. The dove landed there in a tree and spoke in a human voice, commanding that an shrine and oracle to Zeus be built in that location.
  • mihi: = "by me"
  • Clīō: Clīō, Clīūs (a Greek form of the gen.) sing. fem. nom; PrN of one of the goddesses know as the Muses. Clio is often considered the goddess of the writing of history
  • vallibus: ABL(X) without prep. can = "in X"
  • Ascra: Ascra, Ascrae, fem. PLN of a small town in Boeotia, a region of Greece. Here Hesiod, the inventor of didactic poetry, was watching over his sheep when the Muses appeared to him and gave him the gift of poetry.
6

Ūsus opus movet hoc: vātī pārēte perītō;

Vēra canam: coeptīs, māter Amōris, ades!

  • Ūsus: = "experience"
  • pārēte: verb frame = SBJ is obedient to X, where X is DAT
  • perītō = skilled/experienced
  • canam: poems are tradionally referred to as "sung"
  • coeptīs: = "undertakings"
  • ades: verb frame = SBJ provides help/support to X, where X is DAT
7

Este procul, vittae tenuēs, insigne pudōris, 15

Quaeque tegis mediōs, instita longa, pedēs.

  • Este procul: Roman religious ceremony would begin with the demand that all who did not belong there should leave
  • vittae: a strip of cloth tied around the head. It was worn by women who Ovid does not "want" to read his How To book: wives and younger, unmarried women.
  • instita: a reference to married women. The instita seems to have been a band of material sewn to the bottom of the long gown (stola) typically worn by a Roman matron. The purpose of the instita was to protect the finer fabric of the stola from damage from friction with the ground and the woman's shoes.
8

Nōs Venerem tūtam concessaque furta canēmus,

Inque meō nūllum carmine crīmen erit.

  • Venerem: here the PrN of the goddess is used to indicate a love affair
  • concessa: = "permitted"
  • furta: = "thefts" = love affairs carried on secretly/by deceit
  • carmine: = "poem"
9

Principiō, quod amāre velīs, reperīre labōrā,

Quī nova nunc prīmum mīles in arma venīs.20

  • Principiō: ABL(X) without prep. can = "at/in X." Ovid is explaining the organization of his work.
  • quod amāre velīs: a rather dehmanizing expression, "what you may desire to love/have as a lover"
  • velīs: verb frame = SBJ wants/desires to X, where X is a complementary infinitive clause
  • labōrā: verb frame = SBJ works/makes and effort to X, where X is a complementary infinitive clause
  • Quī: this relative has no expressed antecedent. It therefore introduces what is called an "autonomous relative clause." In English, it is often convenient to translate with "whoever" instead of "who"
  • prīmum: adverb = "for the first time"
  • mīles: secondary predication = "as a miles"
  • in arma venīs = "to take up arms"; Ovid describes arranging love affairs as a kind of war, perhaps with his eye on the strategy and tactics required
10

Proximus huic labor est placitam exōrāre puellam:

Tertius, ut longō tempore dūret amor.

  • Proximus: Proximus + DAT(X) = "next to X"
  • exōrāre: an infinitive can be the SBJ of est, as in "to err is human"
  • placitam: = "decided on"; placitam ... puellam is the is the "girl" one has decided to persue as a lover
  • Tertius: = "the third (task)"
  • ut ... dūret: an ut + SUBJ clause may function as SBJ or PNOM of the verb est (here elided)
  • tempore: ABL(X) without prep. can = "for/for the duration of"
11

Hic modus, haec nostrō signābitur ārea currū:

Haec erit admissā mēta premenda rotā.

  • modus: = "bounds/limit." Ovid claims that he is restricting his treatment to the three topics just mentioned.
  • signābitur: Ovid compares his work to a race: this space (ārea) will be marked by his chariot.
  • admissā ... rotā: can be taken as an ABL absolute = "with the wheel having been loosed" = "when the race starts"
  • mēta: one of the turning posts at each end of a chariot course. A good charioteer might try to keep as close as possible to the metae to ensure the shortest route
  • erit ... premenda: passive periphrastic, debitive: "SBJ will have to be verbed"
12

Dum licet et lōrīs passim potes īre solūtīs, 25

Ēlige cui dīcās 'tū mihi sōla placēs.'

  • lōrīs: = "reigns"; ABL(X) without prep.= "with X"
  • cui dīcās: this relative clause has no expressed antecedent. Thus, it is an autonomous relative clause and it has the syntactic function that would have been carried out by the (non-existent) antecedent. In English, we usually provide an indefinite antecedent: "anyone to whom ..."
13

Haec tibi nōn tenuēs veniet dēlapsa per aurās;

Quaerenda est oculīs apta puella tuīs.

  • dēlapsa: as a circumstantial participle, the perfect deponent of a verb X is often translated "having -EN(X)" not "having been -EN(X)"
  • aurās: the English idiom is singular, "(out of) thin air"
  • Quaerenda est: passive periphrastic of a verb X= "SBJ must be -EN(X)"
  • oculīs: a (deliberately) ambiguous structure:
    • DAT(X) dependent on quaerenda = "with/by means of X"
    • DAT(X) dependent on apta = "suitable for X"
