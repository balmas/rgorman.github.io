More Selections from Ovid's Ars Amatoria

More about the theater: refer to treebank, #39-40

1

Spectātum veniunt, veniunt spectentur ut ipsae:

Ille locus castī damna pudōris habet.

  • Spectātum: a poorly understood grammatical structure called the (1st) supine. This "supine" is identical to the mas/neu acc of the perfect participle. However, it is active rather than passive and is generally used to indicate purpose with verbs indicating motion. Here = "to observe (others)"
  • spectentur ut: remember that in poetry a subordinating conjunction may be displaced from the beginning of the clause
  • ut: + subjunctive → a "that/so that" clause
  • damna .. habet: = "has damage of ..." = "is harmful to ..."

Here we omit Ovid's retelling of the story of the Sabine Women. Ovid now summarizes the lasting effects of the ancient events (refer to treebank # 60):

2

Scīlicet ex illō sollemnia mōre theātra

Nunc quoque fōrmōsīs īnsidiōsa manent.

  • sollemnia: in the Roman and Greek world, dramatic performances (and the theater in which they were held) were often associated with the gods and religion
  • mōre: = "tradition"
  • fōrmōsīs: substantized adjective, = "the beautiful"; DAT(X) = for X/for the advantage/disadvantage of X"
  • īnsidiōsa: = "unexpectedly dangerous"

The next recommended place to find "romance" is the Circus (not the one with clowns!) (Refer to treebank # 61-78)

3

Nec tē nōbilium fugiat certāmen equōrum;

Multa capāx populī commoda Circus habet.

  • fugiat: present subjunctive in main clause → volitive; volitive = "an expression of will" = a command, request, plea, etc. English formula = "let the SBJ verb"
  • capāx: = "fit for X" (in the sense of being spacious enough for X), where X us genitive
  • commoda: = "advantageous things" = "advantages"
  • Circus: PLN, the Circus Maximus, a chariot-racing stadium said by some to be large enough for 250,000 spectators.

Ovid now explains some of these advantages to looking for a lover at the Circus

4

Nīl opus est digitīs, per quōs arcāna loquāris, 95

Nec tibi per nūtūs accipienda nota est:

  • Nīl: = nihil; this noun is indeclinable, i.e., it has only one form for all combinations of number and case. In poetry, neuter accusative adjectives often function as adverbials; = "to the extent of nothing" = "not at all"
  • opus est: this combination is best treated as equivalent to a verb: = "there is need of/work for X" where X is ABL
  • loquāris: in relative clauses, the subjunctive verb often indicates a potential (rather than an actual) action/event; this mood serves to generalize; = "may/might communicate"
  • tibi: "by you"; a meaning of the DAT that is common as a dependency of a passive periphrastic verb
  • nota: = "a sign"
  • accipienda est: debitive aspect = SBJ must be + -EN(VERB)
5

Proximus ā dominā, nūllō prohibente, sedētō,

Iunge tuum laterī quā potes usque latus;

  • Proximus ā dominā ...: In the Circus, unlike the theater, there were no strict rules about seating arrangement. Thus women and men could sit together.
  • Proximus: in Latin the expression is "close/closest from X" rather than the English "to X"; proximus here functions as a secondary predication
  • ā: prep. + ABL(X) = "from X"
  • dominā: can be a term of endearment
  • prohibente: Ablative Absolute construction; translation formula = "with SBJ(X) + -ING(VERB)" where X is ABL
  • sedētō: the so-called Future Imperative has an archaic and solemn sense. Cf. the biblical "Thou shalt ..."
  • Iunge: verb frame = SBJ touches X to Y, where X is ACC and Y is DAT
  • quā: a relative adverb introducing an autonymous relative clause; = "where"
  • usque: best translated as a dependency of quā: = "wherever"
6

Et bene , quod cōgit, sī nōlīs, līnea iungī,

Quod tibi tangenda est lēge puella locī. 100

  • bene: ellipsed form of bene est; the ellipsed est needs a SBJ and it is best to assume that this element is provided by the quod-clauses
  • quod cōgit: quod here is a subordinating conjunction rather than a relative pronoun. It can be translated "that the SBJ compels ..." The quod-clause as a whole is the SBJ of bene est.
  • cōgit: verb fram= SBJ compels Y, where Y is a complementary infinitive clause.
  • nōlīs: subjunctive mood = potential event/action
  • līnea: a chalk line on the bench separated individual "seats"
  • iungī: the SBJ of this clause is omitted and must be "restored" anaphorically
  • Quod: another "that-clause" with quod acting as a subordinating conjunction
  • tibi: = "by you/by your agency"
  • tangenda est: present debitive passive = SBJ must be -EN(VERB)
  • lēge: ABL(X) without prep. = from/from the cause of/due to/because of X"
7

Hīc tibi quaerātur sociī sermōnis orīgō,

Et moveant prīmōs pūblica verba sonōs.

  • Hīc: = "here"
  • tibi: DAT(X) can = "by you/by your agency"
  • quaerātur: present subjunctive → present volitive; = "let + BASE(VERB); here the formula for the English passive must also be added
  • sociī: = "friendly" in contrast to "romantic" or "intimate"
  • moveant: present subjunctive → present volitive; = "let + BASE(VERB)
  • pūblica: = something like "suitable for the public" in contrast to "private"
  • verba: Ovid may be implying that words as formed in the mind give rise to the sounds associated with these words
8

Cuius equī veniant , facitō, studiōse, requīrās :

Nec mora , quisquis erit , cui favet illa, favē.

  • This couplet gives an example of how to start a socius sermo
  • Cuius: an interrogative pronoun
  • veniant: what kind of clause is this: interrogative word, subjunctive verb, matrix verb of information processing?
  • facitō: = "do" in the English emphatic sense, e.g, "do tell" or "do go on" etc. The verb frame is [SBJ] do X, where X is a subjunctive clause. The use of the so-called future imperative archaic and solemn.
  • studiōse: = "eager (person)" = "student"
  • requīrās: = "ask"
  • mora: apparently the SBJ of an ellipsed verb sit meaning "let there be"
  • quisquis erit: autonymous relative clause; promoted to the role of its putative antecedent and functions as OBJ of favē
  • cui favet illa: relative clause; either attributive directly dependent on quisquis or, more probably, autonomous and functioning as the predicate nominal of erit
  • favet: verb frame = SBJ supports/favors X, where X is DAT
9

At cum pompa frequēns caelestibus ībit eburnīs , 105

Tū Venerī dominae plaude favente manū;

  • cum: subordinating conjunction, not prep. = "when"
  • pompa: a parade held before the racing
  • frequēns: = "crowded with X" where X is ABL without a prep.
  • plaude: verb frame = SBJ applauds for X, where X is DAT
  • manū: ABL(X) without prep. = "with/by means of X"
10

Utque fit, in gremium pulvis sī forte puellae

Dēciderit, digitīs excutiendus erit:

  • Ut: subordinating conjuction = "as"
  • fit: = "happens"; chariot racing was a dusty business
  • gremium: = "lap"
  • sī: as often in poetry, the subordinating conjunction is shifted from its expected place at the beginning of its clause
  • Dēciderit: = "fall down"; future perfect → completed future = "will have + -EN(VERB); this tense construction can be translated with the English present tense; the use of the future perfect emphasizes the high probability of the event: dust will fall
  • digitīs: ABL(X) without prep. = "with/by means of X"
  • excutiendus erit: → debitive future passive = SBJ will have to be/must be + -EN(VERB)
11

Etsī nūllus erit pulvis, tamen excute nūllum:

Quaelibet officiō causa sit apta tuō. 110

  • nūllum: =the non-existent (dust)"
  • Quaelibet: quīlibet, quaelibet, quodlibet are so-called "free-choice adjectives" = "whoever/whatever/whichever you please"
  • officiō: = "kind service"
  • sit: present subjunctive → present volitive; = "let SBJ BASE(VERB)"
  • apta: = "suitable for X" where X is DAT
12

Pallia sī terrā nimium dēmissa iacēbunt,

Collige, et inmundā sēdulus effer humō;

  • Pallia: = "cloaks"; the plural may refer to the repetition of this action on different occassions
  • terrā: ABL(X) without prep. = "at/in/on X"
  • dēmissa: = "hanging down"
  • Collige: verb frame = "SBJ picks up X" where X is ACC; here X must be understood anaphorically
  • sēdulus: a secondary predication
  • humō: ABL(X) without prep. = "from X"
13

Prōtinus, officiī pretium, patiente puellā

Contingent oculīs crūra videnda tuīs.

  • Prōtinus: = "immediately"
  • officiī: = "kind service"
  • pretium: "reward"; pretium is in apposition to the main clause; the event related in the main clause is therefore equated with/described as an officiī pretium
  • patiente puellā: Ablative Absolute clause; use the formula "with SBJ + -ING(VERB)
  • Contingent oculīs crūra videnda tuīs: one of the more oddly expressed clauses in Ovid
  • Contingent: verb frame = "SBJ fortunately happens to/befalls X," where X is DAT
  • crūra videnda: literally = "legs being seen"; better English would be "a view of her legs"
14

Respice praetereā, post vōs quīcumque sedēbit, 115

Nē premat oppositō mollia terga genū.

  • Respice: verb frame = "SBJ takes care that X" where X is an ut/nē + subjunctive clause
  • post vōs quīcumque sedēbit: autonomous relative clause, promoted to function of its putative antecedent, thus SBJ of premat
  • post: prep. + ACC(X) = "behind/after X"
  • quīcumque: quīcumque, quaecumque, quodcumque, another so-called "free choice adjective/pronoun" = "whoever/whatever/whichever
  • oppositō: = "stuck out/placed in the way
  • terga: again we would expect a singular, but Ovid is describing a general strategy that should be repeated as often as is useful
15
Parva levēs capiunt animōs: fuit ūtile multīs

Pulvīnum facilī composuisse manū.

  • Parva: = "small things"; an attributive adjective non directly dependent on a "noun" takes on the syntactic function of that "noun"
  • levēs: = "light" = "non-serious"; at entertainments such as the races, peoples' spirits may be unencumbered by serious matters
  • fuit ...: an example of a "small thing" that may impress a potential lover
  • Pulvīnum: = "seat cushion"
  • composuisse: = "arrange"; infinitives can often function as the SBJ or PNOM of est; in poetry, the perfect infinitive can be used as the apparent equivalent of the present infinitive
  • facilī: lit. = "easy"; here combines the senses of "friendly" and "dexterous"
  • manū: ABL(X) without prep. = "with/by means of X"
16

Prōfuit et tenuī ventōs mōvisse tabellā,

Et cava sub tenerum scamna dedisse pedem. 120

  • Prōfuit: verb frame = "SBJ(X) is useful/advantageous" where X is a infinitive clause
  • et ... Et: "both ... and"
  • ventōs: = "winds" = "breezes"; again, the plural may represent the repetition of this strategy on many occasions
  • mōvisse: probably equivalent to present infinitive
  • tabellā: = "racing form/program"; ABL(X) without prep. = with/by means of X
  • cava: the meaning of this attribute is unclear; perhaps scamna were "hollow" to reduce weight for portability
  • sub: prep + ACC(X) = "beneath/under X"
  • scamna: = "foot stools"; on the plural see ventōs, above
  • dedisse: = "provide"; in the tense, see mōvisse, above
17

Hōs aditūs Circusque novō praebēbit amōrī,

Sparsaque sollicitō trīstis harēna forō.

  • aditūs: = "approaches/opportunities"
  • Circusque: -que ... -que = "both ... and"; here two SBJs of the verb are conjoined
  • praebēbit: verb frame = "SBJ offers/presents ACC(X) to DAT(Y)
  • Sparsa: = "sprinkled" (PERF PART of spargō, spargere, spārsī)
  • trīstis: = "sad/sorrowful" because of the associated violence and death
  • harēna: = "sand"; often used to refer to the location of gladiatorial combat
  • forō: ABL(X) wihtout prep. = "at/in/on X"; gladiatorial combats were often held in temporary arenas set up in a market place
18

Illā saepe puer Veneris pugnāvit harēnā,

Et quī spectāvit vulnera, vulnus habet.

  • harēnā: ABL(X) without prep. = "at/in/on X"
  • quī spectāvit vulnera: autonomous relative clause, promoted to syntactic role of its "antecedent"
  • vulnera: literal = "wounds" of the gladiators
  • vulnus: figurative = "a wound" inflicted by the puer Veneris
19

Dum loquitur tangitque manum poscitque libellum 125

Et quaerit positō pignore, vincat uter,

Saucius ingemuit tēlumque volātile sēnsit,

Et pars spectātī mūneris ipse fuit.

  • Dum: subordinating conjunction = "while"
  • loquitur: verb frame = "SBJ converses/talks"
  • manum: here = "(her) hand";
  • poscit: verb frame = "SBJ asks for ACC(X)"
  • libellum: = "program/list of fighters"
  • quaerit: = verb frame = "SBJ asks INDIR_QUEST(X)" where X meets the criteria of the IQ clause
  • positō: ABL ABS; use the formula "with SBJ[ABL(X)] having been + -EN(VERB)"
  • pignore: = "a bet/wager"
  • uter: an interrogative adjective/pronoun, = "which of two/the two"
  • Saucius: secondary predication
  • tēlum: a general term applying to a projectile weapon, such as an arrow or a spear
  • volātile: = "flying"
  • mūneris: lit. = "a public service", often = "a gladiatorial show+ put on by a public official
  • spectātī mūneris: lit. = "watched service" = "the show he had watched" or the like
20

Quid, modo cum bellī nāvālis imāgine Caesar

Persīdās indūxit Cēcropiāsque ratēs? 130

  • Quid: often used emphatically, to draw attention to a statement; = "what about that time ... ?" or the like
  • modo: = "recently"; the event referred to occurred in 2 BCE
  • cum: subordinating conjunction = "when"
  • imāgine: = "imitation/representation"; ABL(X) without prep. = "because of/due to X"
  • Caesar: Augustus
  • Persīdās: = "Persian"
  • Cēcropiās: Cēcropius, Cēcropia, Cēcropium, = "pertaining to/connected with Cecrops," the mythical first king of Athens; = "Athenian"
21

Nempe ab utrōque marī iuvenēs, ab utrōque puellae

Vēnēre, atque ingēns orbis in Urbe fuit.

  • Nempe: = "of course"; such "discourse adverbs/particles" are often used to make the tone more friendly. Here the implication is probably something like "I am not telling you something you don't know (manslaining??), but just providing context for what I am about to say"
  • ab: prep. + ABL(X) = "from X"
  • utrōque: uterque, utraque, utrumque (genitive = utrīusque; for the form, see the document on irregular adjective patterns) = "both/each of two"
  • utrōque marī: the Etruscan Sea and the Adriatic Sea
  • Vēnēre: alternative PERF IND ACT 3rd PLUR of veniō
  • orbis: = "the earth/world"
22

Quis nōn invēnit turbā, quod amāret, in illā?

Ēheu, quam multōs advena torsit amor!

  • Quis: interrogative pronoun, = "who?"
  • turbā: = "crowd"
  • quam: = "how"; a qualifying adverb: is dependent on an adjective or another adverb
  • advena: = "a stranger/foreigner"; the gender of this noun is c. (= "common," applying to either biological male or female); it is often seems to function as an adjective, = "foreign"
  • torsit: = "torture/torment"

The next venue to be discussed is the dinner party

23

Dant etiam positīs aditum convīvia mēnsīs: 135

Est aliquid praeter vīna, quod inde petās.

  • positīs ... mēnsīs: ABL Absolute; use the standard formula: "with SBJ[ABL(X)] having been + -EN(VERB)"
  • aditum: = "opportunity"
  • positīs: Roman tables could be set up and taken down as needed
  • mēnsīs: = "tables"
  • praeter: prep. + ACC(X) = "beyond/in addition to X"
  • vīna: as often, Ovid uses a plural where a singlar would make as good sense
24

Vīna parant animōs faciuntque calōribus aptōs :

Cūra fugit multō dīluiturque merō.

  • faciunt: verb frame = "SBJ(X) makes OBJ[ACC(Y)] OCOMP[ACC(Z)]"; example: "that makes me angry" = SBJ(that) makes OBJ(me) OCOMP(angry)
  • calōribus: DAT(X) can be used as a dependent of an adjective to further specify its meaning; = "for X"
  • Cūra: = "care/worry/seriousness"
  • merō: = "wine"; ABL(X) without prep. = "by means of/with X"
25

Tunc veniunt rīsūs, tum pauper cornua sūmit,

Tum dolor et cūrae rūgaque frontis abit. 140

  • rīsūs: "(bursts of) laughter"
  • cornua: = "horns," here apparently a symbol of rash and bold behavior
26

Tunc aperit mentēs aevō rārissima nostrō

Simplicitās, artēs excutiente deō.

  • aevō: ABL(X) without prep. = "at/in/on X"
  • Simplicitās: = "candor"; in the polite society of the Roman upper classes, it was usual to hide one's thoughts and feelings and not to say what one was thinking
  • excutiente: ABL Absolute; translate with formula: "with SBJ[ABL(X)] + -ING(VERB)"
  • deō: the god of wine = Liber for the Romans
27

Illīc saepe animōs iuvenum rapuēre puellae,

Et Venus in vīnīs ignis in igne fuit.

  • Venus in vīnīs ...: combining physical attraction and drinking is compared to setting fire to what is already burning

Ovid finishes with his first topic and moves on to the next (treebank sentences 98-99).

28

Hāctenus, unde lēgās quod amēs, ubi rētia pōnās,

Praecipit imparibus vecta Thalēa rotīs.

  • lēgās: = "select"
  • Praecipit: verb frame = "SBJ(X) teaches/instructs OBJ[IQ(Y)], where Y is the information transmitted by the act of teaching.
  • imparibus: = "uneven"; the reference is to the difference in length between the hexameter and the pentameter
  • vecta: = "conveyed" by a vehicle; once more ovid is representing creating poetry as driving a chariot
  • Thalēa: more often spelled Thalia outside of Greek; one of the nine Muses; the name means "the flourishing one"
  • rotīs: ABL(X) without prep. = "by/by means of X"; "at, in, on X" is also a possible interpretation
29

Nunc tibi, quae placuit, quās sit capienda per artēs, 155

Dīcere praecipuae mōlior artis opus.

  • tibi: as punctuated, the DAT expresses the agent of sit capienda: = "by your agency/by you"; tibi can also be understood as the OBJ of placuit
  • quae placuit: autonomous relative clause promoted to SBJ of sit capienda; in such cases we may prefer to translate quae as "whoever"
  • placuit: verb frame = ""SBJ(X) pleases/is attractive to OBJ[DAT(Y)]
  • quās sit capienda per artēs: the word order is scrambled, as is common in poetry; prose would have per quās artēs capienda sit
  • Dīcere: verb frame = "SBJ(X) says/declares OBJ[IQ(Y)]; when Y is a clause, an indirect question may be used
  • mōlior: verb frame = "SBJ(X) attempts OBJ[COMP-INF(Y)]"; when Y is a clause, a complementary infinitive may be used;
  • opus: = "a work," especially a "work of art"; this phrase is in apposition to Dīcere
30

Cēra vadum temptet, rāsis īnfūsa tabellīs:

Cēra tuae prīmum cōnscia mentis eat.

  • Cēra: Roman messages were often written on wax tablets
  • vadum: shoal or shallow water; once more Ovid uses a metaphor from sailing
  • rāsis: the wax was inserted into a hollow area scraped in the tablet
  • cōnscia: = "aware of GEN(X)"
  • eat: present subjunctive in main clause → present volitive; what does "volitive" mean?
31

Blanditiās ferat illa tuās imitātaque amantem

Verba; nec exiguās, quisquis es, adde precēs.

  • ferat: present subjunctive in main clause → present volitive
  • imitāta: deponent here = "SBJ(X) copies/imitates OBJ[ACC(Y)]"
  • amantem: = "(a person) loving/a lover"
  • nec: negatives can be tricky, since it is often difficult to decide their scope. Here the negative in nec is best taken as dependent directly on exiguās rather than as negating the whole main clause
  • quisquis es: autonomous relative clause; lacking an antecedent, it is promoted to the putative antecedent's function. However, in this sentence, that function is unclear, although it must be adverbial. The meaning is something like "even if you are/think you are far superior"
  • precēs: = "entreates"
32

Hectora dōnāvit Priamō prece mōtus Achillēs;

Flectitur īrātus vōce rogante deus.

  • Hectora: Greek ACC SING, PrN of a famous Trojan warrior = "Hector"
  • dōnāvit: verb frame = "SBJ(X) gives OBJ-1[ACC(Y)] to OBJ-2[DAT(Z)]"
  • Priamō: DAT SING, PrN of famous Trojan King, father of Hector = "Priam"
  • prece: ABL(X) without prep. = "with/by/by means of X"
  • Achillēs: out of hatred for Hector, Achillies kept and mutilated the dead body of the Trojan
  • vōce rogante: ABL ABS, use formula "with SBJ[ABL(X)] + -ING[VERB(PRES PART)]
  • deus: = "a god"; it is apparently a general reference
33

Prōmittās facitō: quid enim prōmittere laedit?

Pollicitīs dīves quīlibet esse potest.

  • Prōmittās: = "make promises"
  • facitō: facitō + PRES SUBJUNCT is used to emphasize a command, = "do base[SUBJUCNT(X)]"; cf. English "do tell" and "do be quiet"
  • prōmittere = "to make promises"
  • quid: ACC = "what?" Introduces a direct question
  • laedit: verb frame + SBJ[COMP-INFIN(X)] harms OBJ{ACC(Y)]"
  • Pollicitīs: = "promises"; ABL(X) without prep. = "with/by/by means of X"
  • quīlibet: NOM SING = "whoever you please," a so-called "free choice" indefinite pronoun
34

Spēs tenet in tempus, semel est sī crēdita, longum:

Illa quidem fallāx, sed tamen apta dea est.

  • tenet: = "lasts/endures"
  • in tempus: lit. = "into a time," better in English as "for a time"
  • semel: adverb = "once/a single time"
  • crēdita: = "trusted in/relied upon"
  • Illa: anaphorically → Spēs
  • apta: = "suitable/effective"
35

Sī dederīs aliquid, poteris ratiōne relinquī:

Praeteritum tulerit, perdideritque nihil.

  • Here using the language of commerce, Ovid again looks at romantic relationships as transactional
  • ratiōne: ABL(X) without prep. = with/by means of X; here ratio implies both general "reason" and, as a business term, a calculation of profit or loss
  • relinquī: if a relationship is transactional, the party that has profitted first, may reasonably initiate a break up
  • Praeteritum: ≈ "what has happened in the past"
  • tulerit: ≈ "will have earned"; this is another business term, meaning to write something in an account book
36

At quod nōn dederīs, semper videāre datūrus:

Sīc dominum sterilis saepe fefellit ager:

  • You should not let your partner realize that you don't intend to fulfill your promises
  • quod nōn dederīs: autonomous relative clause
  • dederīs: perfect subjunctive in relative clause → potential sense → generalizing effect
  • videāre: present subjunctive in main clause →simple present volitive
  • datūrus: = daturus esse, the future active infinitive → prospective <time from matrix verb> active; since matrix verb is present, translate daturus as prospective present; clause type = indirect statement
  • Sīc: Ovid gives examples of the behavior now called "the fallacy of sunk costs": a logical error where people continue investing time, money, or effort into a decision, project, or activity solely because they’ve already invested so much into it
  • dominum: = "owner" of the farm
  • fefellit: verb frame = SBJ(X) deceives/cheats OBJ[ACC(X)]
37

Sīc, nē perdiderit, nōn cessat perdere lūsor,

Et revocat cupidās ālea saepe manūs.

  • Sīc: a second example of the behavior, the gambler
  • nē: nē + subjunctive often expresses a negative purpose → "so that SBJ(X) not VERB(Y)"
  • perdiderit: perfect subjunctive → completed potential = "may not have lost"; a relatively rare construction; here the completed aspect brings to mind the tallying of profit or loss at the end of a day's gambling
  • cessat: verb frame = SBJ(X) ceases OBJ[Comp-INF(Y)]
  • ālea: recall Caesar's famous words ālea iacta est = "the die is cast"
38

Hoc opus, hic labor est, prīmō sine mūnere iungī;

Nē dederit grātīs quae dēdit, usque dabit.

  • Hoc opus: we must assume the verb est
  • iungī: = "to get together," a (somewhat) indirect reference to sex; an infinitive is often used as SBJ or PNOM of est
  • dederit ... dēdit, ... dabit: Ovid continues to speak of relationships, and sex in particular, as a transactional matter: the woman will continue to sleep with her lover in the hope that her (emotional) investment will pay off
  • Nē dederit grātīs: nē: nē + subjunctive often expresses a negative purpose → "so that she not have given..."
  • quae dēdit: autonomous relative clause
39

Ergō eat et blandīs perarētur littera verbīs,

Explōretque animōs, prīmaque temptet iter.

  • eat et ... perarētur: a rhetorical technique called hysteron proteron, in which the natural or logical order of events is reversed; the name is Greek hysteron = "later" and proteron = "earlier"
  • eat: present subjunctive → simple present volitive
  • perarētur: = "inscribe"; present subjunctive → simple present volitive
  • verbīs: ABL(X) without prep. = "by means of/with X"
  • Explōret: present subjunctive → simple present volitive
  • prīma: = "first"; a secondary predication
  • temptet: present subjunctive → simple present volitive
40

Litterā Cȳdippēn pōmō perlāta fefellit,

Īnsciaque est verbīs capta puella suīs.

  • Cȳdippēn: fem acc sing (a Greek form), a PrN; read her story here.
  • pōmō: ABL(X) without prep. = "at/in/on X"
  • perlāta: = "carried"
  • Īnscia: inscius, inscia, inscium = "unaware"; probably a secondary predication
  • verbīs: ABL(X) without prep. = "with/by means of/by X"
41

Disce bonās artēs, moneō, Rōmāna iuventūs,

Nōn tantum trepidōs ut tueāre reōs;

  • bonās artēs: = rhetoric/skill at public speakinug
  • moneō: a parenthetical insert which does not effect sentence structure
  • iuventūs: = "youth/young people"; vocative case
  • tantum: = "only"
  • ut tueāre: ut + subjunctive → "that-/so that-clause"; often used to express purpose of matrix clause
  • reōs: = "defendant" = "someone accused of a crime in a court of law
42

Quam populus iūdexque gravis lēctusque senātus,

Tam dabit ēloquiō victa puella manūs.

  • Quam ... tam; these are correlative words; in effect, the quam clause explains/gives content to the tam
  • Quam: = "as/just as..."; the verb dabit must be "understood" here from the parallelism between clauses
  • populus: = "the people" = the Roman people as a political unit
  • lēctus: = "select/elite"
  • Tam: = "in that way/ so"
  • dabit ... manūs: = "give hands" = "applaud"; compare the English "to give someone a big hand"
  • ēloquiō: ABL(X) without prep. = "by/by means of X"
43

Sed lateant vīrēs, nec sīs in fronte disertus;

Effugiant vōcēs verba molesta tuae.

  • lateant: present subjunctive in main clause → simple present volitive
  • vīrēs: = "power/strength"; although plural, usually translated with singular
  • sīs: present subjunctive in main clause → simple present volitive
  • in fronte: ≈ "openly/ostentatiously"
  • disertus: = "eloquent"; a conversation should not seem like a speech
  • Effugiant: present subjunctive in main clause → simple present volitive
  • vōcēs: = "conversations"
44

Quis, nisi mentis inops, tenerae dēclāmat amīcae?

Saepe valēns odiī littera causa fuit.

  • nisi: we must "understand" a verb for this clause
  • mentis inops: ≈ "out of his mind"
  • dēclāmat: = "to make a speech/to speak with oratorical bluster"
  • amīcae: DAT(X) = "to X"
  • valēns ... littera: Ovid's meaning is not clear here; perhpas the idea is that of "coming on too strong"
45

Sit tibi crēdibilis sermō cōnsuētaque verba,

Blandā tamen, praesēns ut videāre loquī.

  • present subjunctive in main clause → simple present volitive
  • sermō: = "conversation"
  • cōnsuēta: = "customary"; i.e., don't use rare and learned words to show off
  • -que verba: we must "understand" a verb for this clause
  • ut videāre: ut + subjunctive → "so that-/that-clause"
  • praesēns: = "face-to-face"; Ovid's meaning is unclear: if the ut clause explains blanda, perhaps the flattering words should focus on the woman's appearance; if the ut clause refers more generally to the situation in the matrix clause, perhaps it means "to have a personal conversation" rather than give a speech
46

Sī nōn accipiet scrīptum, inlectumque remittet,

Lēctūram spērā, prōpositumque tenē.

  • scrīptum: ≈ "your text/letter"; literally = "the thing written"
  • inlectum = "unread"
  • Lēctūram: = Lēctūram esse, the so-called future infinitive; this form as prospective aspect but gets its tense from a matrix verb = "is about to/going to read"
  • spērā: verb frame = "SBJ(X) hopes OBJ[INDIR-STATEMENT(Y)]"
  • prōpositum ≈ "[your] purpose/committment"
47

Tempore difficilēs veniunt ad arātra iuvencī,

Tempore lenta patī frēna docentur equī:

  • Tempore: = "by means of/in time"
  • iuvencī: = "bullocks/young bulls"
  • lenta: = "pliant"
  • frēna: = "reins/bridles"
  • docentur: verb frame = SBJ(X) teaches OBJ1[ACC(Y)] OBJ2[COMP_INF_CL(Z)]; in the passive, OBJ1 is promoted to SBJ
48

Ferreus adsiduō cōnsūmitur ānulus ūsū,

Interit adsiduā vōmer aduncus humō.

  • adsiduō: = "constant/continuous"
  • ānulus: a finger ring
  • ūsū: = "use/wear"; ABL(X) without prep. = "by/by means of X"
  • Interit: = "is ruined/destroyed"
  • humō: ABL(X) without prep. = "by/by means of X"
49

Quid magis est saxō dūrum, quid mollius undā?

Dūra tamen mollī saxa cavantur aquā.

  • magis: qualifier adverb = "more"
  • saxō: ABL(X) without prep. = "than X"
  • mollius: comparative
  • undā: ABL(X) without prep. = "than X"
  • aquā: ABL(X) without prep. = "by/by means of X"
50

Pēnelopen ipsam, perstā modo, tempore vincēs:

Capta vidēs sērō Pergama, capta tamen.

  • Pēnelopen: FEM ACC SING, PrN of a famous Greek woman = Penelope
  • perstā modo: perinthetical clause; does not affect grammar of matirix clause
  • Capta: = capta esse → completed [tense] passive; tense comes from matrix clause
  • vidēs: verb frame = SBJ(X) sees OBJ[INDIR-STATEMENT_CL(Y)]
51

Lēgerit, et nōlit rescrībere? cōgere nōlī:

Tū modo blanditiās fac lēgat usque tuās.

  • Lēgerit: perfect subjunctive in main clause → completed present volitive or simple present volitive; here the volitive has concessice force as in this example, "Let the rumors spread; the truth will prevail." We could paraphrase as "Even if the rumors spread ..."
  • nōlit: present subjunctive in main clause → simple present volitive; here with concessive force
  • nōlī: the imperative of nōlō ("not want/refuse/be unwilling") is often use in negative volitive sense; thus "refuse to do X," where X is complementary infinitive rather than "don't do X," where X is an imperative
  • fac: emphatic use of the imperative + subjunctive ≈ "do let SUBJ(X) verb(Y)" where Y is subjunctive
  • lēgat: = "read"
