  2. Erat ob has causas summa difficultas

    3. The main kernel is easy, but ...
    • The words ob has causas point to some context to supply meaning for has.
    • The meaning is provided through apposition.
    • Compare the following sentence:
      • There was difficulty because of one man, Marcus Brutus.
    • Here, after a pause indicated by the comma, the words one man are clarified with another phrase, Marcus Brutus.
    • This additional phrase may be considered parallel to one man rather than subordinated as if it were an ATR
    • Such a structure is called apposition.
    • In our Latin sentence note:
      • ob has causas is an ADV expressing cause.
      • Thus in appossition to ob has causas is a causal sub-tree introduced by quod.
      • This sub-tree is complicated with many subordinate clauses, but altogether it can be considered an equivalent of ob has causas.

  3. Erat ob has causas summa difficultas , quod [ADV] , [ADV] [ADV] et [ADV]

    4. The first co-ordinated causal sub-tree.

  4. [[quod]] naves propter magnitudinem [ADV] constitui non poterant

    5. This clausal sub-tree itself contains an ADV sub-tree.
    Note that there is more than one way to handle an ellipsis introduced by nisi.
    • You can say "if ... not" with the ellipsed elements restored (as in this tree).
    • You can say "except ..." and omit the ellipsed elements ("except in deep water").

  5. [[quod]] naves propter magnitudinem ni -si in alto constitui non poterant

    6. Another clausal sub-tree.
    This one consists of three coordinated clauses.
    This is a case of nested coordination: ((non poterant), (desiliendum, consistendum, pugnandum))

    Several points must be kept in mind:
    • Verbs in Latin which take ACC direct objects can make normal "personal" passives
      • Brutus Caesarem interficit -> Caesar [a Bruto] interficitur, "Brutus kills Caesar" -> Caesar is killed [by Brutus]"
    • Verbs that do not take ACC direct object do not make such personal passives.
      • milites de navibus desiliunt -> [a militibus] de navibus desilitur, "the soldiers jump down from the ships" -> "There is a down jumping from the ships [by the soldiers]"
        • There is no good equivalent in English for this impersonal passive; you must do your best.
        • In the present sentence, these impersonal passives are also gerundives (+ erat).
        • Remember this construction means debitive past: "must be verbed"
        • To express the agent of the verb of this kind of construction, a dative is usually used; hence militibus.
