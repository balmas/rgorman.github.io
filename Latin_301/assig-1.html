  • Caesar in Britanniam proficisci contendit [propterea]

    • The satelite in question has a coordinated structure: it is made of two parallel clauses joined by a coordinating conjuntion (et).
    The structure of each clause is similar: an imperfect verb with a complicated OBJ argument.
    Each OBJ is represented by a place holder [aliquid].

  • Caesar in Britanniam proficisci contendit , quod [aliquid] intellegebat et [aliquid] arbitrabatur

    • The OBJ argument of intellegebat is a clause with a participle verb.
    Remember that a perfect passive participle can be the equivalent of a perfect passive infinitive: subministrata can be the equivalent of subministrata esse.

  • hostibus subministrata auxilia

    • In addition to its arguments, subministrata also has satelites. One specifies subministrata temporally, hence the place holder [tum].

  • [tum] hostibus inde subministrata auxilia

    • Because bellis is ABLATIVE, we know it is a satelite in this context. However, ablative satelites may specify the verb in a wide range of ways, for example, PLACE, SOURCE, CAUSE, MANNER, MEANS. Sometimes the meaning of the ablative word iteself makes this relationship clear, e.g. eo die "on that day." Here the temporal meaning of bellis must be inferred from the wider context.

  • bellis hostibus inde subministrata auxilia

    • As usual, the satelites and arguments may themselves be further specified with their own dependencies.

  • omnibus fere Gallicis bellis hostibus nostris inde subministrata auxilia

  • omnibus fere Gallicis bellis hostibus nostris inde subministrata auxilia intellegebat

  • et [aliquid] arbitrabatur

    • The OBJ is a clause with an infinitive verb (fore); it has an PNOM argument expressed; its other argument (SBJ) must be understood from context.
    There also three satelites, here simply marked with [SATELITE].

  • [SATELITE] [SATELITE] [SATELITE] usui fore

    • It may be surprising to see that the PNOM of fore is a DATIVE. We expect the case to be the same as the (assumed) SBJ, which here would be ACCISATIVE.
    But Latin has a common idiom:

  • usui fore

    • We might say "my dog is a joy/pain." Romans would tend to say "canis meus est gaudio/molestiae" ("My dog is for joy/pain.") In other words, my dog contributes TO JOY, etc.
    Here are a few examples:

  • ea res magnae laudi fuit .

    • This idiom often includes a second and separate DATIVE constituent, which seems to specify the interested person, etc.

  • nos sumus voluptati tibi .
  • mihi autem non minori curae est res publica .
  • imperium ipsis principibus prosperum vel exitio fuit .

    • Now we can handle the DATIVE PNOM.
