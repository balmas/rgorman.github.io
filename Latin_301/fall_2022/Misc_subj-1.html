Latin 301
Miscellaneous Uses of the Subjunctive

1
nihil est, quod metuās.
The SUBJ metuās has a potential sense and generalizes the reference.
2
vereor, nē valeat Antōnius.
After vereor we expect a fearing clause. Remeber nē + SUBJ in fearing clause is translated positively: "that." Antōnius is a personal name (PN).
3
nēmō est, quī tē nōn metuat.
The SUBJ metuat has a potential sense and generalizes the reference.
4
nihil erat, quod ad tē scrīberem.
The SUBJ scrīberem has a potential sense and generalizes the reference.
5
simul, nē interclūderer, metuēbam.
With metuēbam we expect a fearing clause.
6
verentur, nē illud senātūs cōnsultum fīat.
With verentur we expect a fearing clause. The active verb fīat is effectivly the passive of facit.
7
metuō, nē magnō reī pūblicae mālō vēnerit.
With metuō we expect a fearing clause. The words magnō mālō are a secondary predication (remember the "don't go away mad construction").
8
timēbam enim, nē ēvenīrent ea, quae accidērunt.
With timēbam we expect a fearing clause.
9
equitātum, quī sustinēret hostium impetum, mīsit.
The SUBJ sustinēret has a potential sense. It expresses not what the horse persons are doing, but what they might/could/should do. Such subjunctives in relative clauses often express the purpose of the action of the matrix verb.
10
ego vērō, quem fugiam, habeō, quem sequar, nōn habeō.
The SUBJs fugiam and sequar have a potential sense and generalize.
11
Cūriō praemittit equitēs, quī prīmum impetum sustineant.
The SBJ sustineant has a potential sense; its clause is meant to express a purpose associated with the antecedent.
12
sī manet, vereor, nē exercitum firmum habēre nōn possit.
With vereor we expect a fearing clause. The phrase exercitum firmum means somthing like "a reliable army."
13
lēgātōs Lacedaemonem mīsērunt, quī Lȳsandrum accūsārent.
The SBJ accūsārent has a potential sense; its clause is meant to express a purpose associated with the antecedent. Lacedaemonem: remember that an ACC w/o a preposition can mean "to x" when x is the name of a city or small island.
14
Rōmae, vereor, nē ex Kalendīs Iānuāriīs magnī tumultūs sint.
With vereor we expect a fearing clause.
15
dum reliquae nāvēs eō convenīrent, ad hōram nōnam expectāvit.
With an IND dum generally means "while." With a SBJ it usually means "until."
16
īrātum mihi Caesarem esse, cum īdem amīcus esset Pompēiō, nōluī.
Recall that the conjunction cum meaning "since" or "because" can introduce a IMPERFECT or PLUPERFECT SBJ that has the same meaning as the corresponding IND.
17
hīs cum persuādēre nōn possent, lēgātōs ad Dumnorigem Aeduum mittunt.
For cum + SUBJ, see note 16. The second argument of persuādēre is usually a DAT. Dumnorigem is a PN. Aeduum refers to his nation.
18
Aeduī, cum sē suaque ab iīs dēfendere nōn possent, lēgātōs ad Caesarem mittunt.
For cum + SUBJ, see note 16.
19
litterās ad tē, numquam habuī, cui darem, quīn dederim, et ab tē, ut idem facias, petō.
The SUBJ darem has a potential sense and generalizes the reference. The conjunction quīn is roughly equivaent to ut nōn; in certain cicumstances (as here) a clause introduced by quīn can be translated into English "without" + gerund: "without giving/sending."
20
in fīnis Lingonum diē quārtō pervēnērunt, cum nostrī eōs sequī nōn potuissent.
Lingonum is the name of a people. For cum + SUBJ, see note 16.
21
etsī, quod scrīberem, nōn habēbam, tamen Canīniō ad tē euntī nōn potuī nihil dare.
The SUBJ scrīberem has a potential sense and generalizes the reference. Canīniō is a PN.
22
ego autem, cum esset incertum iter Caesaris, Leptam mīsī ad Pompeium et litterās.
For cum + SUBJ, see note 16. Leptam and Pompeium are PNs.
23
ego mēcum praeter Dionȳsium ēdūxī nēminem nec metuō tamen, nē mihi sermō dēsit.
Dionȳsium is PN. The conjunction nē introduces a fearing clause.
24
tum dēmum Ariovistus partem suārum cōpiārum, quae castra minōra oppugnāret, mīsit.
Ariovistus is PN. The SUBJ oppugnāret has a potential sense and the relative clause indicates a purpose associated with the antecedent.
25
in quō ūnum nōn vereor, nē tū parum perspiciās ea, quae gesta sunt, ab aliīs esse gesta.
The relative quō here can be translated as "that/this." The conjunction nē introduces a fearing clause.
26
quod enim tū āfuistī, vereor, ut satis dīligenter āctum in senātū sit dē litterīs meīs.
quod is the subordinating conjunction "because." The conjunction ut introduces a negative fearing clause.
27
nesciō enim, cūr cum portam Flūmentānam Caelius occupārit, ego Puteolōs nōn meōs faciam.
For cum + SUBJ, see note 16. faciam is the verb of an Indirect Question. Here it has a potential sense: "why I may not make..." The frame of faciam here takes 3 arguments: x makes y z (SBJ makes OBJ OCOMP). Compare the English structure "that makes me angry."
28
frūmentī magnum numerum coēgit, quod Massiliēnsibus, item quod Afrāniō Petreiōque mitteret.
Massiliēnsibus is the name of a people. Afrāniō and Petreiō are PNs.
The SUBJ mitteret has a potential sense and the relative clause indicates a purpose associated with the antecedent.
29
ex eō locō ab mīlibus passuum octo ventō tenēbantur, quō minus in eundem portum venīre possent.
The phrase quō minus (often written as a single word) is equivalent to ut nōn and with the SBJ indicates a negative result/complement.
30
nēmō ferē vestrum est, quīn, quem ad modum captae sint ā Mārcō Mārcellō Syrācūsae, saepe audierit.
The conjuction quīn here functions as the relative quī + nōn. The SUBJ audierit has a potential sense and generalizes. The phrase quem ad modum = "how."
31
hīs rēbus cognitīs, explōrātōrēs centuriōnēsque praemittit, quī locum idōneum castrīs dēligant.
cognitīs is the verb of an absolute clause.The SUBJ dēligant has a potential sense and the relative clause indicates a purpose associated with the antecedent.
32
haec cum agerentur, nūntiī praemissī ab rēge Iubā vēnērunt, quī illum adesse cum magnīs cōpiīs dīcerent.
For cum + SUBJ, see note 16.
The SUBJ dīcerent has a potential sense and the relative clause indicates a purpose associated with the antecedent.
33
quā rē nūntiātā, Pīrustae lēgātōs ad eum mittunt, quī doceant nihil eārum rērum pūblicō factum cōnsiliō.
nūntiātā is the verb of an absolute clause. quā is anaphoric refering beyond the sentence boundaries and can be translated "this/that." The SUBJ doceant has a potential sense and the relative clause indicates a purpose associated with the antecedent. Pīrustae is the name of a people.
34
Cassius in Thessaliam cum legiōne pervēnit, et hīc cum essent factiōnēs duae, variā voluntāte cīvitātum ūtēbātur.
For cum + SUBJ, see note 16. Cassius is PN. Thessaliam is a place name. The frame of ūtor is x uses/takes advantage of y, where x is NOM and y is ABL.
35
Trēverī vērō atque Indutiomarus tōtīus hiemis nūllum tempus intermīsērunt, quīn trāns Rhēnum lēgātōs mitterent.
Trēverī is the name of a people. Indutiomarus is PN. Rhēnum is a place name (river). quīn ... mitterent can be translated "without sending ..."
36
complūrīs interim lēgātiōnēs Pharnacēs ad Domitium mittit, quae dē pāce agerent rēgiaque mūnera Domitiō ferrent.
Pharnacēs and Domitium/Domitiō are PNs. The SUBJs agerent and ferrent have a potential sense and the relative clauses indicates a purpose associated with the antecedent.
37
ea quī cōnficerent, Gāium Trebōnium lēgātum relinquit, et ipse, ut quam prīmum iter faceret, Cenabum Carnūtum proficīscitur.
The SUBJ cōnficerent has a potential sense and the relative clause indicates a purpose associated with the antecedent. Gāium Trebōnium is PN. The phrase quam prīmum is a idiom meaning "as soon as possible." Cenabum Carnūtum is a place name.
38
cohortibus decem ad mare relictīs et equitibus trecentīs, quī praesidiō nāvibus essent, dē tertiā vigiliā ad hostīs contendit.
relictīs is the verb of an absolute clause. The SUBJ essent has a potential sense and the relative clause indicates a purpose associated with the antecedent. praesidiō ... essent means "might be for protection." We might prefer "give protection to" or the like. dē tertiā vigiliā means "after the third watch."
