Latin 301
Prelude to war: Caesars's evidence

1
Litteris a Fabio Gai Caesaris consulibus redditis aegre ab his impetratum est summa tribunorum plebis contentione ut in senatu recitarentur;
  • Fabio is PN: Fabius
  • litteris ... redditis is an ABL ABS clause. Use the formula "with the SBJ having been EN-form(verb)".
  • impetratum est: the relevant frame of impetro is [SBJ brings X to pass] where X is a ut/ne + subjunctive clause (see lexicon, section I.b). In the passive, X takes the SBJ role.
2
ut vero ex litteris ad senatum referretur, impetrari non potuit.
  • In this context, referretur means something like "be put to a vote."
  • The phrase ex litteris seems to fall under definition III.H. of ex: "To designate the measure or rule, according to, after, in conformity with which any thing is done." We can assume that the letter contained proposals that Caesar expected to be put before the Senate for a vote.
  • impetrari: for the relevant verb frame, see sentence 1.
3
referunt consules de re publica incitate.
  • Again referunt means "put the question," "put up for a vote."
4
Lucius Lentulus consul senatui reique publicae se non defuturum pollicetur, si audacter ac fortiter sententias dicere velint; sin Caesarem respiciant atque eius gratiam sequantur, ut superioribus fecerint temporibus, se sibi consilium capturum neque senatus auctoritati obtemperaturum;
  • pollicetur: the frame of polliceor is [SBJ promises X] where X is an idirect statement clause.
  • defuturum: the frame of desum is [SBJ fails in their duty to X], where X is dative. defuturum is "short" for defuturum esse--it is functionally an infinitive.
  • sententias: in the context of senatorial debate, sententia means "official judgment/decision."
  • dicere: of the may "meanings" given for dico, something like "declare" is good here.
  • velint, respiciant and sequantur are perhaps to be taken as potential. The use of the subjunctives can also be explained by the fact that these verbs are subordinated to indirect statements.
  • respiciant here has a frame [SBJ takes care of the concerns of X] where X is ACC.
  • ut is the comparative relative adverb "as."
  • consilium capturum: see the dictionary entry for consilium II.A.1.b.(a): "to form a purpose or plan, to resolve, decide, determine."
  • sibi: "for himself/for his own benefit."
  • obtemperaturum: the verb frame is [SBJ obeys/submits to X] where X is DAT.
5
habere se quoque ad Caesaris gratiam atque amicitiam receptum.
  • This sentence continues to recount the speech given by Lentulus. We can assume that the infinitive habere is dependent on a verb of speaking implied by the context. In such cases, we could add such a verb or, alternately, just treat habere as the main verb.
  • habere here may have the meaning "consider/think."
  • receptum: functionally the same as receptum esse and thus a perfect passive infinitive. Along with the OBJ prepositional phrase introduced by ad, we may translate something like "... has been recieved into ..."
6
in eandem sententiam loquitur Scipio:
  • in: Like many prepostitions, in has a range of meanings, not all local or temporal. For this passage, meaning II.C is most appropriate: "In other relations, in which an aiming at, an inclining or striving towards a thing, is conceivable, on, about, respecting; towards, against; for, as; in, to; into." We might therefore translate in eandem sententiam as "in support of the same opinion."
7
Pompeio esse in animo rei publicae non deesse, si senatus sequatur;
  • esse: again, this infinitive is treated as the main verb for the purpose of the sentence tree. This practice is not unusual for a string of quasi-idependent indirect statement clauses.
  • desse: for this verb see sentence 4.
  • sequatur is a potential subjunctive.
8
si cunctetur atque agat lenius, nequiquam eius auxilium, si postea velit, senatum imploraturum.
  • imploraturum: = imploraturum esse and is treated as the main verb in the tree diagram. The relevant frame of imploro is [SBJ begs for X] where X is ACC.
  • cunctetur and the other subjunctives may be taken as potential.
9
Haec Scipionis oratio, quod senatus in urbe habebatur Pompeiusque aberat, ex ipsius ore Pompei mitti videbatur.
  • Scipio was Pompey's son-in-law.
  • quod: causal subordinator
  • Pompeius aberat: Pompey held proconsular imperium; he would have to set this power aside if he came into the city.
  • mitti: with an appropriate object, mitto can mean "to emit words."
  • videbatur: as noted above, the passive of video has a special frame: [SBJ seems X] where X is an infinitive (indirect statement clasue). We usually translate the X-clause with an English to-clause.
10
dixerat aliquis leniorem sententiam, ut primo Marcus Marcellus, ingressus in eam orationem, non oportere ante de ea re ad senatum referri, quam dilectus tota Italia habiti et exercitus conscripti essent, quo praesidio tuto et libere senatus quae vellet decernere auderet;
  • dixerat: of the many meaning given for dico, "expressed/declared" works well here.
  • aliquis: and indefinite adjective/pronoun; although singular here, it is better translated as a plural: "some."
  • ut implies an elipsis that must be represented in the diagram: Marcellus must be the subject of some verb that must be inferred from the context.
  • Marcus Marcellus is a PN.
  • ingressus is a circumstantial participle. The relevant frame of ingredior is [SBJ begins X] where X is the preposition in + ACC.
  • in eam orationem: the speech referred to is made more precise by putting the indirect statement non oportere ... in apposition to orationem.
  • in eam orationem: eam is, as usual, endophoric. Here it is cataphoric rather than anaphoric, since it points to the indirect statement that specifies orationem.
  • oportere: the relevant frame of oportet is [SBJ is fitting/proper] where SBJ is an infinitive clause.
  • referri: for the meaning of this word, see sentence 2.
  • quam ("than") is a subordinating conjunction introducing a comparison clause. It specifies the preceding ante. The two words ante and quam may be translated together as "before."
  • quo praesidio: ABL ABS; translate something like "with which as guard."
  • auderet: the relevant frame of audeo is [SBJ dares X] where X is an infinitive clause, usually translated with an English to-clause.
11
ut Marcus Calidius, qui censebat, ut Pompeius in suas provincias proficisceretur, ne qua esset armorum causa;
  • ut Marcus ...: the relative adverb here implies two elipses in the diagram: a main verb upon which the ut clause may depend; and a verb of the ut clause itself, for which Calidius is the SBJ. We may translate ut as "for example."
  • censebat: the relevant frame of censeo is [SBJ proposes/votes for X in the Senate ] where X is an ut/ne + subjunctive clause.
  • qua: an alternative form of of the indefinite adjective quae (qui, quae, quod); translate "any."
12
timere Caesarem ereptis ab eo duabus legionibus, ne ad eius periculum reservare et retinere eas ad urbem Pompeius videretur;
  • timere: an indirect statment continuing the speech of Calidius. As the "highest" verb in this sentence, timere is treated as the main verb in the diagram. The relevant frame of timeo is [SBJ fears X] where X is a ne/ut + subjunctive clause. Remember that in such "clauses of fearing" ne is translated as a positive fear (e.g., "I fear that something is happening").
  • ereptis ab eo duabus legionibus: ABL ABS; translate according to the formula "with [SBJ] haveing been [EN-form(verb)]."
  • duabus legionibus: During a crisis in the east, Pompey had used his political influence to move two legions from Caesar's army to his own.
  • eas: anaphoric.
  • videretur: the relevant frame of videtur (special use of passive of video) is [SBJ seems X] where X is an infinitive clause (indirect statement). Since here reservare and retinere are present infinitives they can be translated as progressive in aspect, "seems to be holding them back and keeping them in reserve..."
13
ut Marcus Rufus, qui sententiam Calidi paucis fere mutatis verbis sequebatur.
  • ut: the relative adverb here implies two elipses in the diagram: a main verb upon which the ut clause may depend; and a verb of the ut clause itself, for which Rufus is the SBJ. We may translate ut as "for example."
  • Marcus Rufus is a PN.
  • paucis fere mutatis verbis: ABL ABS; translate according to the formula "with [SBJ] haveing been [EN-form(verb)]."
  • fere: when this adverb specifies an approach to a higher quantity from "below", it can be translated "almost/nearly," as fere mille ("almost a thousand"). When a lower quantity is approached from "above," the English "merely/just" is more appropriate: pauca fere ("merely/just a few").
14
hi omnes convicio Luci Lentuli consulis correpti exagitabantur.
  • Lucius Lentulus is a PN.
  • correpti: circumstantial participle. corripio probably here has a meaning akin to "swept away" (I.B.2).
  • exagitabantur: exagito here has the meaning "scold/criticize" (II.B.1).
15
Lentulus sententiam Calidi pronuntiaturum se omnino negavit, Marcellus perterritus conviciis a sua sententia discessit.
  • Lentulus is a PN.
  • Calidius is a PN.
  • pronuntiaturum - pronuntiaturum esse and is functionally a future (prospective) infinitive.
  • omnino: a focus particle/adverb. We may translate "at all" or with an similar emphatic exression.
  • negavit: the relevant frame of nego is [SBJ denies X] where X is an infinitive clause (indirect statement) that is usually best translated with an English that-clause.
  • perterritus: a circumstantial participle
16
sic vocibus consulis, terrore praesentis exercitus, minis amicorum Pompei plerique compulsi inviti et coacti Scipionis sententiam sequuntur: uti ante certam diem Caesar exercitum dimittat; si non faciat, eum adversus rem publicam facturum videri.
  • compulsi: a circumstantial participle.
  • inviti et coacti: secondary predication; "they follow unwilling and under compulsion."
  • sententiam ... uti ... videri: sententiam is specified by several clauses in apposition.
  • facturum: here = facturum esse. Of the many meanings listed for facio, the best fit is "to act in a certain manner" (II.A) That manner is here specified by "adversus rem publicam."
  • videri: as noted above, the passive of video has a special frame: [SBJ seems X] where X is an infinitive (indirect statement clasue). We usually translate the X-clause with an English to-clause.
17
intercedit Marcus Antonius Quintus Cassius tribuni plebis.
  • intercedit: this verb is used of the use of the tribunes' veto.
  • Marcus Antonius and Quintus Cassius are PNs.
18
refertur confestim de intercessione tribunorum.
  • In this context, refertur means something like "be put to a vote."
  • Short and easy: do the next one as well.
19
dicuntur sententiae graves;
  • sententiae: the formal opinions of the Senators.
20
ut quisque acerbissime crudelissimeque dixit, ita quam maxime ab inimicis Caesaris collaudatur.
  • ut ... acerbissime ... dixit: the English idiom prefers the comparative, for example, "the more harshly ... anyone spoke..."
  • quam maxime: "as much as possible."
21
Misso ad vesperum senatu omnes, qui sunt eius ordinis, a Pompeio evocantur.
  • Misso ad vesperum senatu: ABS ABL; "with the [SBJ] having been [EN-form(verb)]."
  • misso: see definition II.G for mitto: "dismiss."
  • ad vesperum: "at evening."
22
laudat promptos atque in posterum confirmat, segniores castigat atque incitat.
  • in posterum: "for the future."
23
multi undique ex veteribus Pompei exercitibus spe praemiorum atque ordinum evocantur, multi ex duabus legionibus, quae sunt traditae a Caesare arcessuntur.
  • ordinum: ordo can refer to military rank (II.B.3); here we might translate as "promotions/commands."
  • ex duabus legionibus: see sentence 12.
24
completur urbs et ipsum comitium tribunis, centurionibus, evocatis.
  • completur: the verb frame of compleo is [SBJ fills X with Y] where X is ACC and Y is ABL. In the passive, SBJ is replaced by X.
25
omnes amici consulum, necessarii Pompei atque ii, qui veteres inimicitias cum Caesare gerebant, in senatum coguntur.
  • necessarii: "friends/relatives" from the more genreal meaning of the adjective (II.): "contected with another by natural or moral ties."
  • inimicitias cum Caesare: in Latin, enmities, as well as friendships, can be specified as "with someone" (cum + ABL).
26
quorum vocibus et concursu terrentur infirmiores, dubii confirmantur, plerisque vero libere decernendi potestas eripitur.
  • plerisque: plerusque, pleraque, plerumque is an adjective meaning "very many/most."
  • libere decernendi: a gerund clause.
  • decernendi potestas: "power of deciding..."
  • eripitur: the verb frame for eripio is [SBJ takes X away from Y] where X is ACC and Y is DAT. In the passive, SBJ is replaced by X.
27
pollicetur Lucius Piso censor sese iturum ad Caesarem, item Lucius Roscius praetor, qui de his rebus eum doceant;
  • Lucius Piso and Lucius Roscius are PNs.
  • doceant: a subjunctive with potential force. It expresses not what Roscius is doing, but what he may do. Thus, qui ... doceant may be understood as giving Roscius's intentions and translated as a purpose clause with "so that he ..." instead of "who ..."
28
sex dies ad eam rem conficiendam spatii postulant.
  • conficiendam: when used without an auxilary form of sum, the future passive participle is usually called a gerundive (not a gerund!!). Gerundives have no tense but are passive in voice: "being completed." Gerundives agree with their subjects in gender, number, and case.
  • ad eam rem conficiendam: literally "towards that thing being completed."
29
dicuntur etiam ab nonnullis sententiae, ut legati ad Caesarem mittantur, qui voluntatem senatus ei proponant.
  • sententiae: again refers to the senators' formal recommendations.
  • ut ... mittantur: Language is flexible, and nouns can be further specified by other kinds of clauses than the relative clause. Here sententiae is modified by what we might call a "that-clause" (Latin grammarians prefer to call it an "indirect command clause"). Compare the English "there is a rule that all students must attend Latin class." Clearly the funciton of the that-clause in this example is to give more information about the noun phrase "a rule." Similarly, the ut-clause is categorized as attributive (i.e. specifying a substantive) in the tree.
  • proponant: subjunctive with potential meaning. It indicates not what the legati are doing, but what they may do. Thus, qui ... proponant may be understood as expressing the intention for which the legati may be sent. "So that they ..." is an acceptable translation and better Englishing than "who may ..."
  • proponant: the frame of propono (II.C) is [SBJ reports X to Y] where X is ACC and Y is DAT.
30
Omnibus his resistitur omnibusque oratio consulis, Scipionis, Catonis opponitur.
  • resistitur: the frame of resisto is [SBJ opposes X] where X is DAT. In the passive, the verb is inpersonal (there is no real subject), and it may be translated "there is opposition" or the like.
  • opponitur: the frame of oppono is [SBJ speaks X against Y] where X is ACC and Y is DAT. In the passive, SBJ is replaced by X.
  • Scipio and Cato are PNs.
31
Catonem veteres inimicitiae Caesaris incitant et dolor repulsae.
  • Cato is a PN.
  • inimicitiae Caesaris: the genitive is flexible/underdetermined. We would probably say "hostilities toward Caesar."
32
Lentulus aeris alieni magnitudine et spe exercitus ac provinciarum et regum appellandorum largitionibus movetur, seque alterum fore Sullam inter suos gloriatur, ad quem summa imperii redeat.
  • Lentulus is a PN.
  • aeris alieni: = "another person's money." In other words, a loan or a debt.
  • appellandorum: a gerundive clause; passive in voice with no tense, "being named/appointed."
  • regum appellandorum: "of kings being named/appointed."
  • largitionibus: = "bribes."
  • gloriatur: the relevant frame is [SBJ boasts X] where X is an indirect statement clause.
  • Sullam: Lucius Cornelius Sulla had ruled as dictator a few decades previously.
  • summa: = "the whole" of something.
  • summa imperii = "the whole of power" = "supreme command."
  • redeat: a potential subjunctive. Not "is coming" but "may come."
