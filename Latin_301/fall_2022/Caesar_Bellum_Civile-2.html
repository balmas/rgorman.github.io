Caesar takes action

1
Quibus rebus cognitis Caesar apud milites contionatur.
  • Quibus: the antecedent of this relative adjective is in previous sentence. In such a case, English prefers the demonstrative "these/those" to the relative "which."
  • cognitis: the verb of a ABL ABS clause; use the translationese formula.
2
omnium temporum iniurias inimicorum in se commemorat;
  • in se: se is reflexive. What does that mean?
3
a quibus deductum ac depravatum Pompeium queritur invidia atque obtrectatione laudis suae, cuius ipse honori et dignitati semper faverit adiutorque fuerit.
  • a quibus: the antecedent of this relative pronoun is in previous sentence. In such a case, English prefers the demonstrative "these/those" to the relative "whom."
  • deductum ac depravatum: = deductum esse ac depravatum esse.
  • queritur: the relevant frame of queror is [SBJ complains (that) X] where X is an infinitive (indirect statement) clause.
  • faverit: the relevant frame of faveo is [SBJ promotes X] where X is DAT. faverit is SUBJUNCTIVE because it is subordinate to an indirect statement clause.
4
novum in re publica introductum exemplum queritur, ut tribunicia intercessio armis notaretur atque opprimeretur, quae superioribus annis armis esset restituta.
  • introductum: = introductum esse.
  • exemplum: the meaning of this noun is further specified by the ut + SUBJUNCTIVE clause ("that" clause) that is in apposition to it. Translate something like "pattern that ... intercession may be censured ..."
  • queritur: for the frame of queror, see notes to sentence 3.
  • notaretur: if this is the correct reading, meaning II.C is the relevant one: "to censure."
5
Sullam nudata omnibus rebus tribunicia potestate tamen intercessionem liberam reliquisse;
  • omnibus rebus: "in all affairs" or the like.
  • nudata: the head of an ABL ABS clause; translate with the usual formula.
  • reliquisse: the "main verb" of this sentence is an infintive, as often happens when someone's words or thoughts are being reported. If you prefer, you can assume that a verb like queritur has been elided. The relevant frame of relinquo is [SBJ leaves X Y] where X and Y are both ACC. The apposite definition is I.3.B, "to leave a thing behind in a cerain state."
6
Pompeium, qui amissa restituisse videatur bona, etiam, quae ante habuerint, ademisse.
  • habuerint: the verb of a relative clause subordinate to an indirect statement is often SUBJUNCTIVE. In this sentence, the relative clause is autonomous.
  • ademisse: the "main verb" of this sentence is an infintive, as often happens when someone's words or thoughts are being reported. If you prefer, you can assume that a verb like queritur has been elided.
7
quotienscumque sit decretum, darent operam magistratus, ne quid res publica detrimenti caperet, qua voce et quo senatus consulto populus Romanus ad arma sit vocatus, factum in perniciosis legibus, in vi tribunicia, in secessione populi, templis locisque editioribus occupatis;
  • darent operam: these two words can be treated as a single light verb phrase. In such a construction, the verb itself carries little content meaning, which is instead conveyed by the OBJ. Contrast English "he takes a shower" to "he showers." Thus, darent operam = "make an effort" or "try"; operam does not fill the OBJ slot in the verb frame. The true OBJ is usually filled by an ut/ne + SUBJUNCTIVE clause (a "that" or "that ... not clause").
  • quid: = "anything."
  • qua voce = "by which language" = "by which phrasing."
  • factum = factum est or perhaps factum esse.
  • in perniciosis legibus: this and the two following prepositional in + ABL constructions give the circumtances in which the relevant event occured.
  • occupatis: probably best taken as the head of an ABL ABS clause.
8
atque haec superioris aetatis exempla expiata Saturnini atque Gracchorum casibus docet.
  • expiata = expiata esse.
  • Saturnini: a PN; Saturninus was a notoriously seditious tribune.
  • Gracchorum; a PN. The Gracchi were famous brothers who used the tribunate to disturb the status quo.
9
quarum rerum illo tempore nihil factum, ne cogitatum quidem nulla lex promulgata, non cum populo agi coeptum, nulla secessio facta.
  • quarum: the antecedent of this relative adjective is in previous sentence. In such a case, English prefers the demonstrative "these/those" to the relative "which."
  • nihil: can be translated "none."
  • ne X quidem = "not even X".
  • agi: ago here has the frame [SBJ conducts business with X] where with X is a cum + ABL prepositional phrase. When the construction is passive, as here, there is no true subject, and we may translate something like "business is conducted with ..." See definition II.D.8, "Hence the phrase: agere cum populo, of magistrates, to address the people in a public assembly, for the purpose of obtaining their approval or rejection of a thing"
10
hortatur, cuius imperatoris ductu novem annis rem publicam felicissime gesserint plurimaque proelia secunda fecerint, omnem Galliam Germaniamque pacaverint, ut eius existimationem dignitatemque ab inimicis defendant.
  • hortatur: the relevant frame of this (deponent) verb is [SBJ urges X] where X is an ut/ne + SUBJUNCTIVE clause.
  • ductu: ductus = "generalship."
  • gesserint: the relevant frame of gero is [SBJ exerts themselves for/sustains X] where X is ACC. The SUBJUNCTIVE here is common in relative clauses subordinate to indirect statement clauses.
  • pacaverint: also SUBJUNCTIVE as common in relative clauses subordinate to indirect statement clauses.
  • ut ... defendant: an ut + SUBJUNCTIVE clause ("that" clause). Grammars frequently call such a clause a substantive clause or an indirect command clause.
  • defendant: the relevant frame of defendo is [SBJ defends X from Y] where X is ACC and from Y is an ab + ABL prepositional phrase.
