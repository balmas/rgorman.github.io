Latin 301
Prelude to war: Cicero's evidence

1
ego ad urbem accessi pridie Nonas Ianuarias.
In this letter, Cicero recounts his return to Rome after governing the province of Cilicia. It is the fateful year 49 BCE. Nonas Ianuarias: this seems to be a limit construction, "to the January Nones."
2
obviam mihi sic est proditum, ut nihil possit fieri ornatius.
When the adverb obviam is combined with some form of the verb eō "go", it means something like "go to meet." Recall that fieri functions as the passive of faciō: "be done/be made."
3
sed incidi in ipsam flammam civilis discordiae vel potius belli.
With the words vel potius belli, Cicero offers an alternative expression that better fits the situation.
4
cui cum cuperem mederi et, ut arbitror, possem, cupiditates certorum hominum (nam ex utraque parte sunt qui pugnare cupiant) impedimento mihi fuerunt.
cui cum cuperem mederi is an example of interlaced word order. cui refers anaphorically to the situation (civil discord) indicated by the previous sentence. The verb frame (valency) of mederi is "x remedies y" where x is the appropriate case for the SBJ and y is DAT. nam ... cupiant is an parenthetical insert. utraque = "each." qui pugnare cupiant is an atonymous relative clause functioning as SBJ of sunt. impedimento is the so-called dative of cause; translate "cause of hinderance" or simply "hinderance."
5
omnino et ipse Caesar, amicus noster, minacis ad senatum et acerbas litteras miserat et erat adhuc impudens qui exercitum et provinciam invito senatu teneret, et Curio meus illum incitabat.
qui ... teneret is an atonymous relative clause. Its function is to specify adhuc impudens The adverb adhuc occasionally has a comparative sense as "so" in "so good" etc. Thus adhuc impudens means "so shameless". English would prefer something like "that he would keep" for qui teneret. Curio is a PN.
6
Antonius quidem noster et Quintus Cassius nulla vi expulsi ad Caesarem cum Curione profecti erant, postea quam senatus consulibus, praetoribus, tribunis plebis et nobis, qui pro consulibus sumus, negotium dederat ut curaremus ne quid res publica detrimenti caperet.
  • Antonius and Quintus Cassius are PNs. These men were plebeian tribunes at the time related. This fact explains Cicero's claim nulla vi expulsi.
  • Curio is a PN.
  • expulsi functions as a circumstantial participle.
  • The negotium given by the Senate is specified by ut ... caperet. This action is the equivalent of the suspension of the Writ of Habeas Corpus in the US: it is a nullification of the normal protections of the law.
  • quid ... detrimenti = "anything of harm," i.e. "any harm."
7
numquam maiore in periculo civitas fuit, numquam improbi cives habuerunt paratiorem ducem.
Comparatively straightforward.
