Latin 301
Infinitives and gerunds

1
Ego ā cōnsiliō fugiendī absum.
absum, "to be apart from" here has a psychological sense: "to have nothing to do with."
2
Nūllum locum praetermittō agendī.
locum here means something like "opportunity."
3
Etiam illa causa est nōn veniendī.
Etiam means "even" or "also."
4
Nihil necesse est mihi dē mē ipsō dīcere.
The expression necesse est often has an infinitive clause as its SBJ.
5
Difficile est mē id sentīre, quod tū velīs.
The expression difficile est often has an infinitive clause as its SBJ.
6
Difficile est omnia persequī et nōn necessārium.
For difficile est see note 5; nōn necessārium is found with the same kind of construction. Notice that persequī is deponent.
7
Quod ad Xenōnem scrīpsistī, valdē mihi grātum est.
Xenōnem is a PN. quod introduces a "that" clause which is the SBJ of the main verb est. It is similar to the English construction in "That we lost money makes me mad."
8
Loquor enim dē doctō homine, cui vīvere est cōgitāre.
Notice that loquor is deponent. An infinitive clause can be used as SBJ and/or PNOM of the verb est.
9
Negōtium magnum est nāvigāre atque id in mēnse Quīntilī.
An infinitive clause can be used as SBJ of the verb est. The pronoun id here stands for the general sentiment expressed in the first half of the sentence.
10
Ita rēs sē habet, ut ex nātūrā vīvere summum bonum sit.
Ita rēs sē habet, "the matter has itself thus" is a common idiom for something like "the situation is this..." An infinitive clause can be used as SBJ of the verb est.
11
Labor multō maior est tōtam causam, quam partem, dīcere.
An infinitive clause can be used as SBJ of the verb est. multō is a so-called Ablative of Degree of Difference ("by much"). Thus the two words multō maior can be translated "much greater."
12
Ā mē nūllum tempus praetermittitur dē tuīs rēbus et agendī et cōgitandī.
Ā mē indicates the agent of the passive verb praetermittitur.
13
Oppianicī pecūnia post iūdicium factum ab iūdice ablāta est.
Oppianicī is a PN. post iūdicium factum is called a dominant participle construction. It can be translated "after the decision was made."
14
Ad eās ego eī litterās nihil rescrīpsī, nē grātulandī quidem causā.
causā in the ABL most often means "for the sake of." nē ... quidem are focus adverbs surrounding the word they emphasize: "not even x."
15
Neque nunc mittendī tamen ūlla causa fuit praeter eam, dē quā tibi rescrībī voluistī.
Nothing too tricky here.
16
Sequātur necesse est, ut, cuius in potestāte summum bonum, in eiusdem vīta beāta sit.
Less often than a infinitive, the expression necesse est can have a subjunctive clause as its SBJ. Introduce the subjunctive clause with "that": "it is necessary that it follow ..." Sequātur here has the sense of "follow logically," "be logically consistent." The SBJ of Sequātur is likewise here a subjunctive clause, this time introduced by ut.
17
Ego mē in hāc omnī causā facile intellegēbam pugnandī cupidīs hominibus nōn satis facere.
The gerund pugnandī further specifies the adjective cupidīs by telling us what the people are desirous of. Because pugnandī specifies an adjective, it functions here as an adverbial.
18
Post hanc habitam contiōnem duābus tribusve hōrīs optātissimī nūntiī et litterae vēnērunt.
Post hanc habitam contiōnem is a dominant participle construction. Translate "after this gathering was held."
19
Aeduī, cum sē suaque ab iīs dēfendere nōn possent, lēgātōs ad Caesarem mittunt rogātum auxilium.
Aeduī refers to a group of people. Caesarem is a PN. rogātum is a participle (also called a supine) expressing purpose: "to ask for."
20
Nec vērō ipse posteā tempus ūllum intermīsī dē rē pūblicā nōn cōgitandī sōlum sed etiam agendī.
nōn x sōlum sed etiam y is a focus construction: not only x but even y.
