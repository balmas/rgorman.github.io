Classical Latin Pronunciation Guide

A guide for students on the rules of Classical Latin pronunciation.

Vowels

There are five vowels in Latin: a, e, i, o, u. Each can be long or short. The length of a vowel affects its sound. In modern texts, a long vowel msy br indicated with a macron, a horizontal line placed above the letter (e.g., ā, ē, ī, ō, ū), while a short vowel is usually left unmarked.

  • A: Sounds like "ah" in "father." Long (ā): as in "father." Short (a): as in "about."
  • E: Sounds like "eh" in "pet." Long (ē): as in "they." Short (e): as in "end."
  • I: Sounds like "ee" in "machine." Long (ī): as in "machine." Short (i): as in "it."
  • O: Sounds like "oh" in "vote." Long (ō): as in "open." Short (o): as in "object."
  • U: Sounds like "oo" in "flute." Long (ū): as in "flute." Short (u): as in "put."

Diphthongs

Diphthongs are two vowels that are pronounced as a single gliding sound.

  • ae: Sounds like "eye" in "sky."
  • au: Sounds like "ow" in "cow."
  • oe: Sounds like "oy" in "boy."
  • ei: Sounds like "ay" in "grey."
  • eu: Not a common diphthong, but pronounced "eh-oo."
  • ui: Not a common diphthong, often pronounced "oo-ee."

Consonants

Most Latin consonants are pronounced as they are in English, but there are a few important exceptions.

  • c: Always hard, like the "c" in "cat."
  • g: Always hard, like the "g" in "goat."
  • j: A consonant 'j' is not used in classical Latin. The letter 'I' is used as a consonant and is pronounced like "y" (e.g., Iūlius = "yoolius").
  • v: Pronounced like an English "w" in "wine."
  • s: Always unvoiced, like the "s" in "sun."
  • qu: Pronounced "kw," like in "queen."
  • r: Pronounced as a rolled "r" (trilled "r").
  • ph, th, ch: These are often simplified to "p," "t," and "k."

Syllable Division and Accent

  • Syllable Division: A single consonant between two vowels goes with the second vowel (a-mā-tō). Two or more consonants are divided between the vowels (par-tēs).
  • Accent: The accent almost always falls on either the second-to-last (penultimate) or third-to-last (antepenultimate) syllable.
    • If the penultimate syllable is long, it gets the accent.
    • If the penultimate syllable is short, the accent moves to the antepenultimate.
